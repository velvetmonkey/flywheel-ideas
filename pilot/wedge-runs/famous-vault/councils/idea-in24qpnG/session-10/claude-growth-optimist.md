## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Fire Phone succeeds because Amazon solved the wrong problem with the right lever: subsidy. The load-bearing assumption—that Prime integration plus hardware differentiation pulls switching—held not because consumers chose Fire *for* Prime, but because Amazon was willing to eat hardware losses as a Prime-member retention tax. By mid-2015, Fire had captured 5-7% of the Prime base (4-6M accounts). These weren't power-users; they were price-sensitive households in the $300-500 income bracket who would have churned to Android anyway. Amazon kept them. The assumption's real strength: it never depended on Fire *replacing* iPhone; it only needed to prevent Android defection in the mid-market. Firefly + Dynamic Perspective became table-stakes features for a third OS, not differentiators, but they were good enough. App developers, seeing real volume, shipped apps. The ecosystem didn't explode, but it didn't collapse either. Year two, Amazon iterated hard on performance and camera. By 2016, Fire Phone acquired 8-10M units cumulative and held them. That's not an iPhone-level win, but it's a beachhead—exactly what Amazon needed to stay credible in the phone space long enough for Alexa to launch and create a hardware platform that actually *did* drive switching.
