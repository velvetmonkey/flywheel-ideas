## Revised stance (Pass 2)

My Pass 1 stance over-credited execution failure and under-credited assumption failure. I attributed the collapse to COVID timing and mobile-only inflexibility as *constraints*, but that framing avoids the harder question: was premium subscription pricing for short-form video ever viable, even pre-COVID? The declared assumption — that a mobile-only product fits on-the-go viewing demand — contains two testable claims that I conflated. (1) *Short-form is consumed during in-between moments:* COVID actually tested this directly. Users sheltered at home, in-between moments collapsed *and* they still consumed short-form (Shorts, TikTok exploded). So the assumption's context shifted, not the demand. (2) *Mobile-only is the right product fit:* This claim *failed outright* in the new context. Quibi couldn't pivot to TV/web, and users apparently didn't value mobile-exclusivity enough to pay for it. But even pre-COVID, subscription pricing for short-form was an untested bet riding on beta selection bias. YouTube Shorts (free) and TikTok (free) proved demand existed; they never proved willingness to pay. Quibi's failure wasn't just bad timing — it was a product locked into an assumption about *where* consumption happens, launched exactly when that assumption's premise (commuting) was about to evaporate, with zero pivot path and unproven unit economics. I claimed 18-month runway + ad pivots would have saved it. That's unfalsifiable counterfactual reasoning masquerading as analysis. The actual evidence is: $1B+, major distribution (Verizon), premium content, and it still failed in 6 months.

## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 was optimism-driven outcome bias. I saw COVID as the *killer* and treated execution failure as redemptive — 'they had the right insight; external event destroyed it.' But that narrative lets me avoid confronting whether the core assumption (premium short-form on mobile, sold to commuters) was ever sound. Three failures in my reasoning: (1) I cited 'measured in beta' user appetite as proof of market fit, but selection bias is invisible in that data — beta users opt-in to test premium products. (2) I used counterfactual reasoning ('with willingness to go ad-supported...') to argue away the failure, but the company actually *couldn't* pivot fast enough, which is itself evidence the assumption was brittle. (3) I'm a Growth Optimist — I see upside narratives and external causes because that's my role. But I'm not accounting for base rates: most subscription video plays fail. Quibi had distribution, budget, content, and still couldn't scale. That's not 'bad timing'; that's 'the bet didn't work.' The premium assumption was never validated at scale. COVID didn't disprove the assumption so much as expose that the assumption was contingent on a specific context (commuting) that was always fragile.
