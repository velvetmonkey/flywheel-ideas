## Initial stance (Pass 1)

asm-pvzvWzNB is the core failure point. The first half of that assumption says premium short-form viewing happens primarily on mobile during in-between moments. I do not buy that. People absolutely use phones in queues, breaks, and commutes, but those moments favor frictionless, free, socially-fed content, not a paid library of TV-budget episodes that require intent, audio, and continuity. Confusing "people look at phones a lot" with "people want premium episodic storytelling on phones" is a category error.

The second half of asm-pvzvWzNB says a mobile-only product fits that demand. That is worse. Mobile-first can be a feature; mobile-only becomes a self-inflicted distribution choke point. It strips out TV fallback, co-viewing, screenshots, casting, and other behaviors that help premium video travel socially and justify subscription pricing. Even if some demand for short premium viewing exists, exclusivity to phone narrows the product exactly where premium media usually needs flexibility.

My stance is strongly negative. This bet loads Hollywood cost structure onto a consumption context optimized for low-commitment, free alternatives. The downside is severe: expensive content obligations, weak retention after trial, dependence on paid marketing instead of organic sharing, and a narrow path to product-market fit that disappears if the in-between-moments thesis is even slightly wrong. A risk council should treat this as a high-burn, low-forgiveness launch with failure modes that compound rather than offset.
