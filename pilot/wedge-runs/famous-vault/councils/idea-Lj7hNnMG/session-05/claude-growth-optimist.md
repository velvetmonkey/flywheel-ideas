## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 was optimism-bias masquerading as analysis. I validated the *demand assumption* in a vacuum and hand-waved execution as solvable. Three failures: (1) I didn't interrogate supply-chain incentives—premium creators have no reason to produce mobile-exclusive when syndication to TV/web/app bundles pays better. The assumption 'premium short-form happens on mobile during in-between moments' is probably *true as a consumer preference*. ## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Mobile-first premium short-form is real—commutes aren't gone, skipping ads for quality matters to aspirational viewers, and $5/month for TV-budget production of *that* aesthetic is a legitimate willingness-to-pay moment. The assumption holds *on its core claim*. Where execution gets interesting: the opportunity isn't just mobile-during-commute but mobile-everywhere—waiting rooms, between Zoom calls, weekend grocery runs. We're not fighting TikTok's time-sink loop; we're displacing cable's fragmented "always watching" posture with intentional, curated consumption. Quibi's bet is smarter than it looks: mobile-only forces discipline on production (5-10min is *hard* to write), which creates scarcity value. The risk isn't the assumption; it's competitive saturation and subscriber accumulation cost, which are solvable with marketing and bundling. Content is sticky if it's genuinely better.

## Key risks

- Creator/studio incentives misaligned: premium talent prefers multi-screen distribution, bundles, international reach over mobile-exclusive contracts
- Unit economics break below $15-20/month subscription price given required production volume and creator compensation
- Mobile-exclusivity prevents viral loops, household penetration, and TV-casting parity that Netflix/Hulu depend on
- Free short-form (TikTok, YouTube Shorts) captures the same demographic with different price; premium positioning requires *scarcity*, but studio contracts won't enforce it
