## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Twelve months later, I think the failure path runs straight through asm-pvzvWzNB. That assumption actually hides three separate bets: that premium short-form becomes a repeat habit, that the habit lives primarily on phones, and that the highest-frequency use case is the in-between moment. If any one of those is only half-true, the model weakens; if all three are merely adjacent to the truth, Quibi ends up exquisitely built for a consumption window that is too small and too fragile to support a paid entertainment service.

Attack on asm-pvzvWzNB directly: maybe viewers do watch short video on mobile, but the in-between moment is dominated by free, social, low-friction feeds, not a subscription decision. Maybe viewers do want premium storytelling, but they want it wherever they are most comfortable finishing it, including TV and laptop, so mobile-first works while mobile-only backfires. And maybe 5-10 minute episodes are not the problem at all; the problem is asking TV-budget economics to depend on stolen moments instead of destination viewing.

My optimistic read is that the market signal was real but misshaped. There was likely upside in premium short-form as a cross-device, hybrid-priced, socially distributable product, or as a feature inside a larger subscription bundle, but Quibi appears to have frozen the narrowest interpretation of demand into the whole product. So what went wrong was not believing in mobile energy; it was mistaking a promising wedge for the entire market and overfitting the service to asm-pvzvWzNB.
