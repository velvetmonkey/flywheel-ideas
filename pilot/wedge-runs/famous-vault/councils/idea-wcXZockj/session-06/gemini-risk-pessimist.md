## Initial stance (Pass 1)

The proposed "residential-house metaphor" for Microsoft Bob represents a superficial and potentially detrimental attempt to simplify computer interaction. While well-intentioned, the mapping of abstract computer functions to physical spaces like "rooms" for applications and "drawers" for folders is inherently prone to misinterpretation and will likely introduce more cognitive load than it alleviates. Novice users will not only need to understand basic computer operations but also decipher the nuances of this imposed metaphor, a double burden that risks alienating them rather than welcoming them. The assertion that this approach will lead to faster task completion than the existing Program Manager is highly dubious; a poorly understood metaphor invariably slows users down. Furthermore, the reliance on "helpers" introduces a variable that is notoriously difficult to get right – they can easily become patronizing, repetitive, or simply unhelpful, exacerbating user frustration. This bet on a quaint spatial metaphor over functional clarity is a high-risk gamble with a substantial probability of failure, leading to an interface that is more confusing and less efficient for its intended audience.

## Key risks

- User confusion and frustration stemming from an opaque or inconsistent metaphor mapping.
- The 'helpers' feature becoming a significant source of annoyance rather than assistance, increasing support burdens.
- Alienation of more experienced users who find the metaphor simplistic and restrictive.
- Failure to achieve market adoption or positive user sentiment due to a perceived lack of genuine utility or ease of use.
- The potential for the metaphor to become outdated or irrelevant as computing paradigms evolve.
