## Initial stance (Pass 1)

The proposed residential-house metaphor for Microsoft Bob, while aiming for novice-friendliness, carries significant inherent risks that could undermine its very purpose. The fundamental assumption that abstract computer functions can be cleanly and intuitively mapped onto spatial, domestic elements like "rooms" and "drawers" is a precarious one. For instance, what constitutes a "room" beyond a mere application launcher? Will users understand that a "drawer" is not just a container for files but also potentially for settings, system utilities, or even temporary data, and how does this vary across different "rooms"? This conceptual leap introduces a layer of indirection that novices, lacking prior computing context, may find more bewildering than liberating.

Furthermore, the scalability of such a metaphor is highly questionable. While basic operations like launching an application or opening a folder might be superficially represented, how will Bob handle more complex workflows, system configurations, or advanced features? The metaphor is likely to break down under the weight of real-world computing tasks, leading to a frustrating experience where users struggle to find functionalities or understand their purpose. Instead of a lower learning curve, we might be creating a gilded cage, where novice users are guided into a simplified, yet ultimately restrictive and confusing, environment that fails to prepare them for the broader computing landscape. This approach risks creating a generation of users who are adept at navigating Bob's house but lost in the wider digital world.

## Key risks

- Metaphorical breakdown for complex tasks leading to user confusion and frustration.
- Inability to scale the metaphor for advanced computer functions and system management.
- Hindrance of genuine computer literacy development by promoting superficial understanding.
- High development cost with a significant probability of low user adoption or severe usability issues.
