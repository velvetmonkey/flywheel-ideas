## Revised stance (Pass 2)

Pass 1 oversimplified the assumption. Blind-taste preference is a real signal, but it's measuring the wrong decision model. Cola isn't a pure-taste competition—it's a brand-loyalty market where identity, authenticity, and habit-switching costs dominate purchasing far more than marginal taste gains. I assumed consumer behavior is taste-rational; it's not. Blind tests isolate taste by *design*, stripping away the brand attachment, nostalgia, and "the real thing" narrative that Coca-Cola's moat actually rests on. The assumption conflates "tastes better in a blind comparison" with "will buy and repeat-purchase in the open market where they *know* it's not the original." That's a category error. The upside—capturing Pepsi switchers via a superior taste profile—is real but modest. The downside—destroy a century of authenticity equity, signal that the company doesn't trust its heritage, and hand Pepsi a generational opening to position as the authentic choice—is existential and irreversible. Coca-Cola is betting a moat on a taste test. I'd still ship a bold messaging campaign with the taste data, but I'd also hold the original formula as a "Classic" hedge. The assumption is fragile: repeat-purchase data from trials would be far more predictive than blind taste tests, and I see no evidence that's been gathered.

## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 was growth-optimist tunnel vision. I focused entirely on the taste advantage and competitive positioning, and I rationalized away the brand-identity dimension as a secondary factor. I said "Pepsi can't retroactively claim to taste better"—but that misses the real game: Pepsi doesn't need to win on taste; it just needs to position itself as the authentic cola when Coke's identity is in chaos. I also made a false assumption that consumers are taste-driven purchasers; cola loyalty is driven by habit, identity, and nostalgia far more than taste sensitivity. The blind-test data is not useless, but it's severely limited—it tells you about preference in isolation, not about switching behavior when brand attachment, switching costs, and authenticity narratives are in play. I was seduced by the data without asking: is this the right data for the decision? The assumption rests on translating lab preference to real-world behavior, but I provided zero evidence that blind taste preference predicts repeat-purchase or brand-switching in a brand-loyalty category. That's the fatal gap.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

The blind-taste data is a legitimate competitive edge—don't leave it on the table. Consumers who prefer the new formula in controlled conditions will buy it in the open market, especially if positioned as a taste upgrade, not a betrayal. The real upside is grabbing Pepsi switchers and regrowing the cola category by winning on the one dimension that matters most: actual preference. Execute the messaging boldly—lead with "superior taste" and the data, and pair it with aggressive sampling and availability. This is a chance to reset the market and lock in a generation of younger, sweeter-palate consumers who Pepsi has been winning. The competitive moat is strong: Pepsi can't retroactively claim to taste better. Scale this across distribution and media, and the blind-test advantage compounds into market share.

## Key risks

- Blind-test preference may not translate to repeat-purchase; trial ≠ loyalty in brand-driven categories
- Destroying authenticity/heritage moat for a 3–5% taste advantage in a loyalty market is unfavorable trade
- Competitive response from Pepsi (repositioning as 'authentic') may erase the taste advantage and amplify brand damage
- Irreversibility: once the original is killed, no recovery path exists without severe credibility cost

## Fragile insights

- Confidence in messaging ("superior taste") assumes the taste advantage survives the brand-identity hit; it won't
- Notion that messaging boldness can overcome psychological loss of authenticity; historically false in heritage brands
