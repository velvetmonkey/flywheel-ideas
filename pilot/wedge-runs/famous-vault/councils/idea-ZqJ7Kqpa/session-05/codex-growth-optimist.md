## Revised stance (Pass 2)

I still think there was real upside hiding in the taste data. If the sweeter formula beat both Pepsi and legacy Coke in large blind tests, Coca-Cola probably found a product with genuine trial appeal. From a growth seat, that matters: most incumbents never get a clean signal that a new variant could widen demand instead of just defending share.

My revised objection is that asm-fCadm4Cw asks one research method to answer several different business questions at once. A blind sip test can show immediate sensory preference. It cannot, by itself, prove branded shelf choice, repeat purchase, willingness to lose the original, or durable loyalty after a public switch. That is my explicit attack on the assumption: it treats a local taste win as if it were a complete demand model.

The counter-argument I had underplayed is that full replacement has strategic clarity. One formula can focus media, bottling, and retailer attention, while a line extension can get stranded as a half-committed experiment. If branded in-market evidence had shown consumers would keep buying after being told the original was going away, I could support the bold move. But with only blind-taste evidence, I still favor harvesting the upside through a parallel launch, regional rollout, or Pepsi-switcher sub-brand rather than replacing the flagship.

I leaned hard on nostalgia and identity, but the sharper critique is methodological: asm-fCadm4Cw collapses trial, substitution, repeat purchase, and tolerance for losing the incumbent into one inference. I also did not fully surface the best pro-replacement case, which is that decisive portfolio simplification can unlock faster adoption than a cautious coexistence strategy.

I may also be leaning on hindsight. Because New Coke became a canonical failure, it is easy to talk as if backlash was obvious in advance. The fairer standard is narrower: what evidence existed at decision time, and did it really justify permanent replacement? On that narrower standard, I still think the assumption was overextended, but not that the sweeter formula itself was obviously a bad growth idea.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

I like the ambition here. If the sweeter formula consistently wins against both Pepsi and legacy Coke in blind tests, there is real upside in using product innovation to retake share instead of just spending harder in advertising. This is one of the few growth bets where the signal is concrete: if consumer palates have shifted, a better-tasting cola could move share at scale.

But `asm-fCadm4Cw` is doing too much work. A blind sip win is not the same thing as open-market purchasing behavior, because the branded experience is part of the product. People do not just buy sweetness; they buy familiarity, ritual, identity, and the promise that the iconic thing they love will still exist tomorrow. If the evidence only proves "preferred in a blind comparison," it has not yet proved "will switch, repurchase, and accept the permanent loss of the original."

My optimistic read is to keep the upside while changing the shape of the bet. Launch the sweeter formula as a parallel product, a regional market test, or a sub-brand aimed at Pepsi switchers instead of replacing the core. That preserves the growth opportunity exposed by the taste data while letting the market answer the real question: whether preference survives branding, shelf context, and repeat purchase. I am positive on the innovation thesis; I would not back total replacement of the flagship on this assumption alone.
