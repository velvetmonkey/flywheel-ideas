## Views

### Risk Pessimist (claude)

Blind-taste preference *can* translate to purchasing behavior, but Coca-Cola's test likely embedded survivorship and consumption-context biases that won't generalize. Single-sip sweetness is not the same signal as sustained-consumption preference—sweetness fatigues, becomes cloying in longer consumption arcs, and context-matters (warm vs. cold, with food, in mixed drinks). The test panel almost certainly skews toward consumers already sensitive to sweetness—precisely the demographic Pepsi targets. Coca-Cola's core base (older, routine drinkers) was likely *underrepresented* in the blind sample or responded differently because their taste buds are less sensitive to marginal sweetness differences. The announcement and forced substitution *do* matter, but not because they erase taste preference—because they create a revealed-preference test among holdouts who defect entirely rather than adapt. Blind preference ≠ loyalty under friction.

**Key risks:**
- Sweetness preference in single-sip lab test does not translate to sustained preference across repeated servings (fatigue, cloying effect)
- Test panel demographic skew toward sweeter-preference consumers underrepresents Coca-Cola's core installed base
- Context-dependent consumption (warm, mixed drinks, with food) not captured in blind test; results may not generalize
- Forced substitution + scarcity create a revealed-preference test; holdouts may defect to Pepsi or private label rather than adapt, even if taste is superior

**Fragile insights:**
- Identity and habit only *override* taste if taste preference was marginal or inconsistent; strong taste preference in blind conditions survives habit friction
- Announcement damage is a *separate* risk from taste assumption; confusing them obscures where the real failure is
- Core demographic preference (older, routine drinkers) in the blind test is an assumption I didn't validate; if they preferred new formula, identity loss becomes less of a factor

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: The blind-test margin was large enough AND the test panel was representative of Coca-Cola's core demographic (older, routine drinkers) such that preference persists across repeated servings and contexts. If the data showed older consumers *also* preferred the new formula, then my 'identity-invested holdout' argument collapses. Also wrong if sweetness preference *does* survive fatigue (i.e., if Coca-Cola's formula was calibrated to a fatigue-resistant sweetness, not peak-sip sweetness). Finally wrong if brand-identity effects on beverage choice are weaker than beverage-identity effects—if consumers care more about 'what am I drinking' than 'who am I'.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw — I underweighted the extent and rigor of the blind-test data and overclaimed the magnitude of expectation effects. The assumption is sound in structure; my vulnerability is in *quantifying* how much taste preference decays in real-world consumption vs. lab, and whether the test sample generalizes.`
- Confidence rationale: 0.58 reflects genuine uncertainty. Pass 1 was ~0.75 reflexive confidence in downside; Pass 2 drops because: (1) I was too quick to dismiss 'extensive' taste-test data; (2) I conflated separate risks rather than cleanly testing the core assumption; (3) repeated-consumption fatigue is a real vulnerability I should have surfaced in Pass 1, but it's also speculative—Coca-Cola may have designed for fatigue-resistance. The stance is still skeptical (taste-lab ≠ market is true in principle), but less confident that the gap is large enough to swallow a strong preference signal.

**Evidence:**
- "Single-sip sweetness preference in lab does not predict multi-serving consumption preference in real use" — Neurogastronomy research: taste fatigue and hedonic decline with repeated exposure (e.g., Rolls et al., sensory-specific satiety)
- "Blind-test panels skew toward consumers already sensitive to marginal taste differences (selection bias)" — Inference: consumers volunteering for taste tests are likely soda-engaged and have refined taste sensitivity; may not represent routine drinkers indifferent to small formula changes
- "Context-dependent consumption (temperature, food pairing, mixing) alters taste perception; not captured in lab" — Standard food-science finding: beverages consumed cold vs. warm, alone vs. with food, show different preference orderings
