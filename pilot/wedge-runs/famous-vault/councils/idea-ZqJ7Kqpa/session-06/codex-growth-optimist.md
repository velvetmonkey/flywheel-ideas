If the sweeter formula is consistently beating both original Coke and Pepsi in blind tests, that means the company has discovered something commercially potent. The mistake is not ambition; the mistake is using that insight to erase the flagship instead of expanding the franchise.

On asm-fCadm4Cw, I would attack the core leap directly: blind-taste preference is not the same thing as open-market purchasing behavior. A sip test isolates sweetness and immediate sensory response; a real purchase bundles identity, habit, memory, packaging, and the reassurance that "Coke" still means the same thing it meant yesterday. Consumers may prefer the new formula in a decontextualized comparison and still reject it once the choice is framed as replacing a cultural artifact.

My pro-growth answer is to keep the upside but change the vehicle. Launch the sweeter recipe as a line extension, a regional rollout, or a Pepsi-counterprogramming sub-brand aimed at younger buyers, then let repeat purchase data decide whether it deserves more share over time. That preserves the emotional equity of the original while still monetizing the research insight instead of burying it.
