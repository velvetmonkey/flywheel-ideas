## Views

### Risk Pessimist (gemini)

Twelve months into the 'new-coke' venture, the predicted catastrophic failure is now undeniable. This outcome was not unforeseen; it was the direct consequence of a singular, fatal assumption: that superior performance in blind taste tests would invariably translate to robust purchasing behavior. This assumption, `asm-fCadm4Cw`, willfully ignored the profound emotional and cultural capital invested in the original Coca-Cola brand. Consumers did not merely prefer a sweeter taste; they rejected the perceived erasure of a nostalgic icon and a symbol of their identity. The market backlash was not about the product's taste profile but about the violation of a deeply ingrained brand relationship. From a risk-pessimist's perspective, this failure highlights the critical danger of prioritizing quantitative sensory data over qualitative human factors like heritage, sentiment, and brand loyalty when dealing with established cultural artifacts. The company's gamble on chemistry over cultural connection proved to be its undoing.

**Key risks:**
- Consumer alienation due to the perceived erasure of brand heritage and cultural significance.
- Widespread public backlash and negative publicity eroding brand loyalty and impacting sales.
- Significant financial and reputational damage stemming from a strategic decision based on an incomplete understanding of consumer behavior.

**Fragile insights:**
- The insight that blind taste test preference accurately predicts aggregate purchasing behavior for established, emotionally resonant brands.
- The assumption that quantitative taste preference data is sufficient to drive major product reformulations of iconic consumer goods.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: The failure was primarily caused by an unforeseen, critical product defect in the New Coke formula itself (e.g., significant shelf-life issues or unintended chemical degradation) that wasn't captured in initial taste tests, or if an exceptionally aggressive and disruptive competitive response from Pepsi at the time fundamentally altered market dynamics independent of consumer attachment to the original Coke. However, the historical record strongly supports the cultural backlash narrative as the primary driver.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw`
- Confidence rationale: High confidence is warranted due to the New Coke failure being a classic, well-documented case study in business strategy that directly illustrates the risks of over-reliance on taste test data without considering brand equity, emotional connection, and cultural significance. The historical outcome serves as strong validation for the pessimistic view that this assumption was inherently flawed. The counter-argument that execution or competitor actions were solely to blame is less compelling than the overwhelming evidence of consumer outcry against the change itself, driven by their connection to the original.

**Evidence:**
- "The overwhelming public rejection of the New Coke formula and the subsequent return of the original formula, demonstrating a disconnect between taste preference and purchasing behavior for established brands." — Historical market analysis and consumer response data from the 1985 Coca-Cola reformulation.

### Risk Pessimist (claude)

The blind-taste assumption is *necessary-not-sufficient*, and I overstated my Pass 1 attack by strawmanning it as all-or-nothing. Taste preference likely has some predictive power for purchase behavior—the research was probably more rigorous than isolated lab tests—but that signal is heavily discounted by brand identity, ritual, and switching costs in cola specifically. The fatal mistake is not that taste preference fails to translate; it's that discontinuing the original **weaponized that failure by eliminating the escape hatch**. If New Coke coexisted with Classic, a modest taste-preference edge (10-15% of some segments) might have produced slow growth over time. By forcing a permanent switch, Coca-Cola converted a partial truth (taste matters) into a binary ultimatum that triggered identity defensiveness and revealed the assumption's insufficiency all at once. My Pass 1 framing conflated two failures: (a) the assumption itself (dubious but possibly directionally correct), and (b) the discontinuation strategy (clearly catastrophic). The taste-preference assumption *could* be true; the execution made it irrelevant.

**Key risks:**
- Taste-preference assumption may be directionally correct (preference does correlate with trial/repeat) but magnitude is misestimated in aggregate due to heterogeneous segmentation favoring heavier original-Coke users.
- Discontinuation of Classic eliminates the possibility of coexistence, forcing a binary choice that reveals customer identity investment rather than testing taste preference fairly.
- In-home use test data and repeat-purchase rates among trial cohorts were potentially strong but Coca-Cola failed to account for nostalgia discount in heavy user segments (older, core customer base).
- Competitive threat from Pepsi (if real and documented) is not addressed by the stated assumption; taste improvement might have been *necessary* but insufficient to stem share loss.
- Younger/lighter-use segments may have genuinely preferred and adopted New Coke in open market, contradicting my assumption that all segments behave like core loyalists.

**Fragile insights:**
- Assumption that brand identity and ritual overwhelm taste preference — true for 60+ age cohort and daily consumers, potentially false for teenagers and non-core users.
- Claim that discontinuation triggered identity defensiveness — plausible but relies on post-hoc attribution; alternative: market conditions or actual taste degradation (formula execution problems, not preference-test validity) were the real culprits.
- Assertion that 49/51 or 40/60 preference split masks heterogeneous retention — assumes the preference data did *not* segment; if Coca-Cola's research team already broke out by usage tier and age, my criticism is redundant.
- Inference that lab context (no brand cues) is the binding constraint — underestimates possibility that in-home trials or demographic-specific studies showed taste preference predictive in some populations.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: Coca-Cola's internal CLT or in-home use-test data showed taste preference was predictive of repeat purchase at >40% correlation in any segment; Classic's reintroduction failed to recover original sales (suggesting taste failure was real); younger consumers or lighter users genuinely preferred New Coke in open market despite identity arguments; competitor reformulations succeeded during the same period (suggesting market-wide taste shift, not Coca-Cola-specific failure).
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw — specifically on *magnitude*. The assumption is directional (preference → behavior), which is likely true in some form. I'm most uncertain about whether the effect size is 5% (trivial), 20% (meaningful), or 50% (material). If taste preference accounted for 20% of the purchase decision in any segment, the assumption is partially validated; I claimed it's near-zero. I don't have data to refute a modest effect size.`
- Confidence rationale: 0.61 reflects: (a) higher-than-even-odds confidence that the discontinuation was catastrophic and identity was a major failure factor; (b) lower-than-comfortable confidence that the blind-taste assumption itself is invalid — it's probably directionally correct but discounted more than Coca-Cola's researchers anticipated; (c) major uncertainty about segment heterogeneity and whether in-home research already accounted for repeat-purchase variability; (d) acknowledgment that I'm leaning on unfalsifiable brand-identity arguments that could be wrong if consumer behavior is less identity-driven and more taste-driven than I assume, especially in non-core segments.

**Evidence:**

- "Discontinuation of original Coca-Cola triggered consumer backlash and identity-based defensiveness, not explained by taste alone." — Historical record (New Coke reintroduced as Coca-Cola Classic ~79 days after launch); the speed of reversal suggests identity/brand trauma, not taste data revision.
- "Blind-taste testing (without brand cues) may underweight factors like packaging, ritual, and social identity that drive cola purchasing." — General principle of choice architecture; specific to cola: Coca-Cola's 79-year brand identity is a material asset not present in blind conditions.

### Growth Optimist (claude)

Pass 1 missed the forest. Blind-taste preference ≠ brand loyalty. I conflated sensory winning with market winning. New Coke's core bet assumes consumers treat cola like pizza—choose on taste. But cola is identity + ritual + heritage. Coca-Cola's *brand value* is the original formula. Testing that in a blind setting removes the actual product. The assumption should have been: "Preference for taste alone, when brand is invisible, predicts behavior when brand is re-introduced." That's a much weaker link. The PepsiChallenge precedent is especially weak—it was a marketing stunt in a constrained context, not evidence of market-wide taste-driven behavior. Where I was right: taste data is clean. Where I was catastrophically wrong: I treated clean taste data as if it answered the actual business question. The business question isn't "which formula tastes better?" It's "do consumers choose cola on taste, or on what cola *means to them?" Those are different. Loss aversion—losing the original—almost certainly outweighs the gain of "better" taste. My execution-risk blindness showed here too: I said "let market mechanics work" without asking whether brand-attachment mechanics are actually favorable. They aren't.

**Key risks:**
- Brand attachment and identity signaling completely invisible to blind taste tests; loss-aversion toward the original formula likely dominates purchase decisions
- PepsiChallenge precedent is weak: it was a limited marketing campaign, not evidence that taste-preference drives market-wide loyalty behavior in cola
- Regional and demographic heterogeneity: sweeter formula may have lost badly in segments not captured by aggregate preference data
- Bottler and distributor resistance not measured; channel partners may have economic incentives to resist, creating friction post-launch

**Fragile insights:**
- Blind taste tests isolate pure sensory preference but remove brand context—the test setup is optimally biased against the variable that matters most
- "Careful execution" framing masks the core problem: if consumers choose cola on heritage, not taste, no amount of positioning fixes the bet
- Taste-optimization assumption may not generalize from marketing campaigns (PepsiChallenge) to market-wide behavior change

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

- Could be wrong if: Ethnographic research reveals that consumers *do* choose cola primarily on taste when aware of a better formula; regional market tests show sweeter formula gaining share despite loss-framing; bottler/distributor channel doesn't resist; brand-attachment measurement shows low emotional attachment to the original formula; or competitors don't amplify backlash.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw — specifically the leap from 'tastes better in blind setting' to 'will be purchased and retained in open market where brand and heritage are visible.' The assumption ignores that Coca-Cola competes on identity, not taste-optimization.`
- Confidence rationale: Dropped from ~0.70 to 0.38 because self-critique exposed a fundamental mismatch: the blind-taste test is the wrong instrument for the actual business question. Pass 1 saw clean data and missed that it answers a different question entirely. The core assumption is fragile because it rests on consumers being taste-optimizers in a category where they are heritage-optimizers. Loss-aversion and brand-attachment effects are likely larger than the taste-preference effect. However, confidence isn't zero because: (1) the taste data itself is valid; (2) a repositioned launch (new variant, not replacement) might capture upside; (3) execution excellence could partially overcome the fragility. But as stated—direct replacement of the beloved original—the bet is nearly certain to fail.

### Growth Optimist (codex)

I still think the most likely 12-month postmortem starts with assumption `asm-fCadm4Cw` failing in the exact place the research looked strongest. A blind sip test measures immediate sensory preference; a market purchase measures identity, habit, trust, and tolerance for change. If the sweeter formula wins in a laboratory comparison but loses once the can says Coca-Cola and the original disappears, then the research was directionally real and strategically misapplied. The company did not prove substitution; it proved that a different recipe can score well in a narrow moment.

My revision after self-critique is that I should attack `asm-fCadm4Cw` even more specifically: it bundles three leaps, not one. First, liking one sip may not predict buying a full serving. Second, initial trial may not predict repeat purchase. Third, preferring the new formula may not imply consent to retiring the old one. Any one of those breaks the chain. In a failure scenario, I would expect the third leap to be the killer because replacement turns product optimization into a symbolic loss for loyal buyers.

The growth-optimist read is that the same data may still have contained a win. A sweeter formula could have been a flank brand, a youth-targeted variant, a fountain-only test, or a regional counter-Pepsi play. So the postmortem is not that the company had no growth signal; it is that it treated a promising expansion signal as a mandate to overwrite the flagship. That is why I still center `asm-fCadm4Cw` as the failure point, while admitting there is a live counter-argument that better launch design or coexistence strategy might have rescued the economics.

**Key risks:**
- Blind taste preference fails to predict named-brand repeat purchase in the open market.
- Replacing the flagship destroys consumer trust even if the new formula performs well as an addition.

**Fragile insights:**
- The same taste-test data may have supported a line-extension win rather than a flagship-replacement win.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

### Risk Pessimist (codex)

Twelve months later, the failure still runs through `asm-fCadm4Cw`. Coca-Cola treated a blind sip win as permission to replace a 99-year-old flagship. That is the kind of inference that looks rigorous in a deck and catastrophic in a market. A sweeter sample can win in a paper cup and still fail once the purchase is public, branded, habitual, and tied to the loss of the incumbent.

I attack `asm-fCadm4Cw` directly. It smuggled four different behaviors into one assumption: momentary liking, shelf choice, repeat purchase, and willingness to surrender the original. The research measured preference without sacrifice. Consumers were not truly forced to confront that preferring the new formula meant the old Coke disappeared. Once management made this a forced replacement, the market stopped answering "which tastes better?" and started answering "why did you take away mine?"

My revised view is harsher but narrower than Pass 1. I was too quick to blame missing brand cues alone. Contemporaneous reporting says identified tests still favored New Coke, which means the deeper failure was not simply unbranded methodology. The deeper failure was assuming that low-stakes taste preference would map onto repeated, emotionally loaded, open-market behavior. That is still `asm-fCadm4Cw`; it just fails on a broader methodological basis.

Work backward from the wreckage and the chain is predictable: large-N taste data creates false certainty, the original is withdrawn, consumers treat the act as subtraction and betrayal, complaints and retailer pressure spike, repeat demand underperforms trial, and management retreats in public. This was not a small forecasting error. It was a flagship replacement built on the wrong behavioral proxy.

**Key risks:**
- Blind-preference research without consequence modeling can justify an irreversible flagship replacement on the wrong metric.
- Removing a heritage incumbent can trigger loss aversion, organized backlash, retailer friction, and reputational damage faster than trial demand can correct the error.

**Fragile insights:**
- Identified or branded tests may still have favored New Coke, so missing brand cues alone may not explain the collapse.
- New Coke might have survived as a line extension; the disaster may belong more to forced replacement than to the sweeter formula itself.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: Archival home-use tests or realistic market pilots showed that consumers were explicitly told the original Coke would be withdrawn, then still bought and repeatedly repurchased New Coke at scale with limited backlash. That would weaken my claim that `asm-fCadm4Cw` itself, rather than rollout theater alone, was the decisive break.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw`
- Confidence rationale: Confidence stays above 0.7 because the historical outcome and contemporaneous reporting both show a clear gap between taste-test preference and market behavior. It is not higher because the exact mechanism is not perfectly isolated: some evidence suggests New Coke could still win identified tests, which means I am more certain that the assumption failed than I am about which sub-mechanism inside that failure dominated.

### Growth Optimist (gemini)

Working backward from this New Coke outcome, it's clear our core assumption – that blind-taste-test preference for the sweeter formula would translate directly to sustained purchasing behavior (`asm-fCadm4Cw`) – was fundamentally flawed. While we, as Growth Optimists, were energized by the data indicating a superior product, we overlooked the profound emotional and psychological layers of brand loyalty. The 'growth' we envisioned was purely transactional, based on taste preference, failing to account for the deep-seated emotional connection consumers had with the original Coca-Cola. This wasn't merely a beverage; it was a cultural artifact, a ritual, a symbol of continuity. By replacing it outright, we didn't just change a taste; we disrupted a deeply ingrained consumer experience, triggering a backlash rooted in loss aversion and brand heritage, not a simple rejection of taste. The true opportunity lay not in replacing what worked, but in strategically augmenting it, leveraging the perceived taste superiority to create an even richer brand ecosystem, a missed growth avenue.

**Key risks:**
- Underestimating the power of brand heritage and emotional consumer connection.
- Misinterpreting taste preference data as a direct predictor of market-driving purchasing behavior.
- Failing to account for consumer sentiment around perceived loss of familiarity and identity.

**Fragile insights:**
- The notion that objective product superiority (e.g., taste) inherently dictates market success.
- The assumption that consumers will readily abandon a beloved, established product for an improved alternative when deep emotional or habitual ties exist.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: Evidence emerged demonstrating that the failure was *exclusively* attributable to poor marketing execution and messaging, rather than a fundamental breakdown in the translation of taste preference to purchase intent. If subsequent reformulations that *added* similar taste profiles but *retained* the original were highly successful, it would imply the issue was the replacement strategy itself, not the core assumption about taste influence. Additionally, if the original Coke's perceived 'icon' status was less prevalent than assumed, and the backlash was due to other factors.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw`
- Confidence rationale: Initial confidence was high, driven by the strength of the blind taste test data and the Growth Optimist's inclination towards data-backed innovation. However, the profound nature of the New Coke failure, rooted in consumer psychology and brand loyalty—factors my persona tends to overlook—significantly reduces confidence. The realization that a technically 'superior' product can fail due to non-rational consumer behavior highlights a critical blind spot.
