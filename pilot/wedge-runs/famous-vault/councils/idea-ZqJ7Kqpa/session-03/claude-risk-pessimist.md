## Initial stance (Pass 1)

The blind-taste-test assumption collapses under scrutiny because it strips away every factor that actually drives cola purchasing. Taste preference measured in an antiseptic lab—no brand, no ritual, no cultural context, no 79-year identity anchor—has zero predictive power for open-market behavior where Coca-Cola's product *is* the mythology, not the liquid. Consumers don't buy taste; they buy belonging. The test removed all the load-bearing elements: nostalgia, 'the real thing' status, the habitual red-can reach. Measuring sensory preference in a vacuum and extrapolating to a population for whom Coke is a citizenship card is a category error.

The second collapse: public discontinuation weaponizes the failure. By yanking Classic off shelves and forcing a permanent switch, the company eliminated the escape hatch and triggered defensive identity—every consumer felt *controlled*, not *served*. If New Coke had been an option *alongside* the original, the test might have mild relevance. Instead, you declared war on habit, and habit won. This isn't a taste failure; it's a brand-management failure disguised as a taste decision.

The third fault line is hidden segmentation. Aggregate blind-preference data masks catastrophic heterogeneity. If 51% prefer New Coke and 49% prefer the original, but the 49% segment is older, core, and buys 10x the volume per capita, the aggregate number is a corpse. Heavy users—the revenue drivers—sit in the 'original' cluster with switching costs that are *impossible*. You collapsed a heterogeneous population into a single preference metric and bet the brand on the tail, not the head.
