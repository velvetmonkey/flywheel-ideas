## Revised stance (Pass 2)

Pass 1 missed the forest. Blind-taste preference ≠ brand loyalty. I conflated sensory winning with market winning. New Coke's core bet assumes consumers treat cola like pizza—choose on taste. But cola is identity + ritual + heritage. Coca-Cola's *brand value* is the original formula. Testing that in a blind setting removes the actual product. The assumption should have been: "Preference for taste alone, when brand is invisible, predicts behavior when brand is re-introduced." That's a much weaker link. The PepsiChallenge precedent is especially weak—it was a marketing stunt in a constrained context, not evidence of market-wide taste-driven behavior. Where I was right: taste data is clean. Where I was catastrophically wrong: I treated clean taste data as if it answered the actual business question. The business question isn't "which formula tastes better?" It's "do consumers choose cola on taste, or on what cola *means to them?" Those are different. Loss aversion—losing the original—almost certainly outweighs the gain of "better" taste. My execution-risk blindness showed here too: I said "let market mechanics work" without asking whether brand-attachment mechanics are actually favorable. They aren't.

## Self-critique (Pass 2)

I saw the signal (blind-test data) and forgot to ask whether I was testing the right variable. The methodology was rigorous but orthogonal to the actual decision. I also anchored heavily on the PepsiChallenge—which succeeded as a *marketing campaign* in *comparative tasting*, not as a predictor of market-wide loyalty shifts. I missed that heritage brands operate on identity, not taste-optimization. I assumed that careful positioning and execution could overcome a fundamentally mismatched core assumption. Execution risk isn't the problem; the assumption is the problem. Execution just makes failure more visible and costly.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

New Coke's bet is sound. Blind-taste-test methodology isolates pure preference from brand noise—exactly what we need to know. The data is clean: across repeated trials, consumers prefer the sweeter formula. This mirrors PepsiChallenge's success; the market has validated that taste preference drives *some* purchasing decisions. Rolling out carefully with a modern positioning (not a replacement story, an advancement) captures the youth segment where sweeter wins hardest. Execution risk is real but manageable—we have scale, distribution, and margin to invest in trial and conversion. The assumption holds if we trust the signal and let market mechanics work.

## Key risks

- Brand attachment and identity signaling completely invisible to blind taste tests; loss-aversion toward the original formula likely dominates purchase decisions
- PepsiChallenge precedent is weak: it was a limited marketing campaign, not evidence that taste-preference drives market-wide loyalty behavior in cola
- Regional and demographic heterogeneity: sweeter formula may have lost badly in segments not captured by aggregate preference data
- Bottler and distributor resistance not measured; channel partners may have economic incentives to resist, creating friction post-launch

## Fragile insights

- Blind taste tests isolate pure sensory preference but remove brand context—the test setup is optimally biased against the variable that matters most
- "Careful execution" framing masks the core problem: if consumers choose cola on heritage, not taste, no amount of positioning fixes the bet
- Taste-optimization assumption may not generalize from marketing campaigns (PepsiChallenge) to market-wide behavior change
