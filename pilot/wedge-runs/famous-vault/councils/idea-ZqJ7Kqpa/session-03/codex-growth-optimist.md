## Initial stance (Pass 1)

The highest-probability postmortem is that we confused winning a sip test with winning a brand choice. Assumption `asm-fCadm4Cw` is the obvious failure point: consumers may genuinely prefer the sweeter formula in a blind comparison and still reject it in the market once the drink is named, packaged, priced, and socially interpreted. A taste test isolates flavor; a purchase decision bundles habit, identity, nostalgia, symbolism, and trust. If this fails, the data was not fake, just aimed at the wrong layer of behavior.

From a growth lens, the tragedy is that there was probably a real opportunity hiding inside the same evidence. The sweeter formula may have been strong enough to expand the portfolio, counter Pepsi in selected channels, or target younger cohorts, but not strong enough to justify deleting the flagship. In other words, we may have found a growth vehicle and mistakenly used it as a replacement vehicle. The failure mode is not “research is useless”; it is “research was over-extrapolated from preference to substitution.”

Working backward from failure at 12 months, I would expect backlash to have compounded through visibility. The more public the replacement, the more consumers read the move as revoking something they owned emotionally. That turns ordinary switching friction into a referendum on the company itself. So my attack on `asm-fCadm4Cw` is direct: even if people liked the new taste, they may not have wanted the original taken away, and that distinction alone is enough to break the business case.
