## Revised stance (Pass 2)

Twelve months later, the failure still runs through `asm-fCadm4Cw`. Coca-Cola treated a blind sip win as permission to replace a 99-year-old flagship. That is the kind of inference that looks rigorous in a deck and catastrophic in a market. A sweeter sample can win in a paper cup and still fail once the purchase is public, branded, habitual, and tied to the loss of the incumbent.

I attack `asm-fCadm4Cw` directly. It smuggled four different behaviors into one assumption: momentary liking, shelf choice, repeat purchase, and willingness to surrender the original. The research measured preference without sacrifice. Consumers were not truly forced to confront that preferring the new formula meant the old Coke disappeared. Once management made this a forced replacement, the market stopped answering "which tastes better?" and started answering "why did you take away mine?"

My revised view is harsher but narrower than Pass 1. I was too quick to blame missing brand cues alone. Contemporaneous reporting says identified tests still favored New Coke, which means the deeper failure was not simply unbranded methodology. The deeper failure was assuming that low-stakes taste preference would map onto repeated, emotionally loaded, open-market behavior. That is still `asm-fCadm4Cw`; it just fails on a broader methodological basis.

Work backward from the wreckage and the chain is predictable: large-N taste data creates false certainty, the original is withdrawn, consumers treat the act as subtraction and betrayal, complaints and retailer pressure spike, repeat demand underperforms trial, and management retreats in public. This was not a small forecasting error. It was a flagship replacement built on the wrong behavioral proxy.

## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 was directionally right but too tidy. I over-attributed the collapse to nostalgia and brand symbolism, which risks missing a stronger counter-argument: New Coke may have been genuinely liked, perhaps even when identified, and the catastrophe may have come more from forced replacement than from the liquid itself. I also underplayed execution variables such as portfolio design, communication, and whether any realistic home-use or market tests ever modeled the loss of the original. My core claim survives, but the mechanism is less "people rejected the taste" and more "management used the wrong experiment to justify an irreversible decision."

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Twelve months later, the failure is not ambiguous: Coca-Cola mistook a laboratory preference for a market truth. The company changed the crown-jewel product, triggered a consumer revolt, and then had to reverse course under public humiliation. That is the signature of a decision built on a narrow metric and an unexamined downside.

The declared load-bearing assumption, asm-fCadm4Cw, is where this breaks. Blind-taste-test preference for a sweeter sip did not translate into purchasing behavior because real purchases are not made in a sensory vacuum. People buy Coke as habit, identity, memory, and ritual; once the company turned a taste test into a forced replacement of the original, it activated loss aversion and betrayal instead of simple preference.

Worse, the research instrument measured the wrong behavior. It captured decontextualized, short-horizon liking, not repeat consumption, not consumer response to eliminating the incumbent, not retailer and bottler consequences, and not the social amplification of backlash. A formula can win in an unmarked cup and still lose in a shopping cart, on a family table, and in public opinion.

Work backward from the wreckage and the sequence is straightforward: management over-trusted taste data, underweighted attachment to the existing formula, and launched an all-or-nothing switch that turned product reformulation into a referendum on corporate judgment. Once consumers decided the company had taken away "their" Coke, the formula itself became secondary. asm-fCadm4Cw failed because preference under blind comparison was never the same thing as willingness to surrender the original and keep buying the replacement.

## Key risks

- Blind-preference research without consequence modeling can justify an irreversible flagship replacement on the wrong metric.
- Removing a heritage incumbent can trigger loss aversion, organized backlash, retailer friction, and reputational damage faster than trial demand can correct the error.

## Fragile insights

- Identified or branded tests may still have favored New Coke, so missing brand cues alone may not explain the collapse.
- New Coke might have survived as a line extension; the disaster may belong more to forced replacement than to the sweeter formula itself.
