## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 leaned too hard on the sheer size of the research program, as if 190,000 to 200,000 tests automatically solved the gap between tasting and buying. That was too generous. A big-N result is impressive, but it does not by itself prove repeat purchase, household replenishment, or tolerance over a full serving. I also used Diet Coke as a stronger analogy than it deserves; launching an additive line extension is materially easier than replacing the flagship.

The strongest counter-argument I underplayed is that consumers may accept an improved variant while still rejecting the symbolic deletion of a ritual product. Put differently: people can like New Coke and still punish Coca-Cola for removing old Coke. I also smuggled in an extra assumption about execution without naming it: that bottlers, retailers, and the launch narrative would convert trial into habit before backlash could crystallize. If that bridge is weak, the whole affirmative case softens quickly.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

If this succeeded 12 months later, the reason is simple and bullish: Coca-Cola correctly diagnosed that the U.S. cola war had become a taste competition disguised as a brand competition. Pepsi had been training consumers to reward sweeter, smoother cola in blind comparison, and Coke finally answered with a formula that beat both old Coke and Pepsi in large-scale testing. In a mature, high-frequency category, a real sensory edge does not need to be dramatic to matter; it just needs to be consistent enough to shift millions of repeat purchase occasions.

Assumption asm-fCadm4Cw held in its strongest form because the blind-test signal was not thin or anecdotal. It was repeated, large-N, and directionally aligned with what the market had already been saying for years. Coca-Cola was not extrapolating from a cute mall stunt; it had months of testing with roughly 190,000 to 200,000 consumers, a clear blind-test win over old Coke, a branded-test win as well, and a favorable comparison against Pepsi. If a formula wins even when the brand is visible, that is the best possible version of the case that taste preference can survive contact with real-world branding and translate into sustained buying.

The supporting pattern is even stronger when you remember that Coca-Cola had recently proven it could extend the trademark successfully when the taste proposition was compelling. Diet Coke, built with extensive consumer research and a taste-led launch, became the No. 1 diet soft drink by the end of 1983 and the No. 3 soft drink overall in the U.S. by the end of 1984. That matters. It says the Coca-Cola name was not fragile glass; it was a distribution-and-habit engine. In this success timeline, bottlers, retailers, and national rollout speed converted superior trial into routine household replenishment before nostalgia could harden into durable resistance.

So the strongest affirmative reading is that New Coke won because Coke stopped romanticizing its legacy recipe and optimized for revealed consumer preference. The formula fit where the market was going, the brand had enough authority to bless the change, and early trial created the repetition needed for preference to become habit. Twelve months later, success would look less like a miracle and more like a disciplined incumbent finally trusting the data hard enough to move first.

## Key risks

- Sip-test and even branded-test wins may not survive full-can, at-home, repeat-consumption behavior.
- Consumers may punish the replacement of an iconic flagship even while preferring the new taste in test conditions.

## Fragile insights

- Diet Coke's success suggests the Coca-Cola trademark can carry a new recipe, but adding a new choice is much easier than deleting a culturally loaded one.
- A branded test win is strong evidence, but it can still overstate real shelf conversion if habit and symbolism matter more than immediate taste.
