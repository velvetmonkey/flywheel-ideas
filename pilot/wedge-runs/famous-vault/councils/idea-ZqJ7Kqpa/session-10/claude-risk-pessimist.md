## Revised stance (Pass 2)

Pass 1 defended New Coke's success by arguing blind-taste preference dominated brand loyalty—but that's backwards. Identity-laden brands (like Coca-Cola) have taste as a SYMPTOM, not a driver. Consumers don't choose beverages via sensory preference in blind conditions; they choose via identity, ritual, nostalgia, and social signaling. The blind-taste test removed the actual decision variables. New Coke succeeded here only if Coca-Cola got extraordinarily lucky: younger demographics broke from family/cultural expectation, marketing suppressed nostalgia narratives, and no competitor seized the opening to resurrect "original." But that's a narrow win, not a natural outcome. The company itself doubted this—they had Coca-Cola Classic in the wings within months. If this 12-month success is real, it's despite the blunder, not because the strategy was sound. Taste didn't dominate; brand structure, demographic generational shift, and market control did. Strip those away—add a vocal "Bring Back Coke" movement, or a competitor offering original formula—and New Coke collapses.

## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 substituted execution competence for strategic soundness. I argued: 'Taste dominated because there was no alternative, execution was flawless, and younger demographics adopted.' But this conflates three separate claims and assumes the hardest one—that blind preference predicts behavior—without evidence. I also assumed marketing suppressed nostalgia narratives indefinitely; in reality, a vocal "Bring Back Coke" movement would emerge within weeks of launch (it did). I credited Coca-Cola's market control as a 'structural advantage' of eliminating choice—but in hindsight, eliminating the original formula was a catastrophic error because it converted preference uncertainty into brand defection risk. If consumers aren't sure they like New Coke, they can't hedge by switching between formulas; they defect entirely. Pass 1 treated manufacturing consistency as a given; it's not—Coca-Cola is complex, and any bottling variation gets blamed on the formula. Finally, I underweighted the possibility that 12-month 'success' reflects demographic luck (younger cohorts trying new things) rather than sustained preference.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

New Coke succeeded because the blind-taste-test assumption held—against my initial skepticism. I expected brand loyalty and nostalgia to override sensory preference; instead, taste proved dominant when consumers faced a binary choice: the new formula or a competitor. Coca-Cola's research isolated the signal cleanly (200,000+ samples, decisive margin) and the rollout executed flawlessly with no distribution gaps or manufacturing drift. Younger demographics, who carried lower emotional attachment to the original formula, adopted readily and habit-formation followed through month 12. The absence of a "bring back the original" hedging product forced consumers to adapt rather than defect—a structural advantage that Coca-Cola leveraged. But I must emphasize the fragility: this success is 12 months old, a short window for brand loyalty to fully crystallize or for taste preference stability to be proven durable.

What troubles me most is how narrowly this hinges on conditions that could fragment. If nostalgia mobilizes—"Bring Back Coke"—brand identity reasserts and taste preference weakens. If manufacturing introduces subtle variation, perceived quality drops below blind-test expectations. If a competitor launches an original-formula product, brand defection accelerates. The assumption required taste to dominate; it did—but only because competing variables were suppressed or absent. Consumer rationality here is fragile: forced adaptation to a single choice, not active preference for the new formula over a spectrum of options. I credit Coca-Cola's market control and flawless execution, not consumer psychology, for this win.

The load-bearing assumption held because blind-taste preference was predictive of behavior—but only in a specific, brittle context: no alternative original formula, distribution perfection, manufacturing consistency, and younger-demographic-led adoption. These dependencies could snap. Coca-Cola has succeeded in the near term by controlling the market structure and eliminating consumer choice. Whether that translates to long-term brand resilience remains untested.

## Key risks

- Blind-taste preference is a terrible predictor of revealed preference in identity-heavy categories; Coca-Cola tested the wrong variable
- Brand loyalty and nostalgia are suppressed, not eliminated—mobilize them and defection accelerates (happens within months, not years)
- Manufacturing consistency at scale is fragile; any variation blamed on formula, not operations; consumers become quality-sensitive
- Demographic adoption isn't adoption—early younger cohorts may simply lack attachment, not affection for new formula; loyalty doesn't follow taste
- Elimination of original formula was punitive, not strategic; forced adaptation breeds resentment, not satisfaction

## Fragile insights

- Taste preference in a controlled setting predicts purchasing behavior in an open market—fundamentally false for branded beverages
- Younger demographics represent a permanent shift, not a temporary adoption cohort that will revert when nostalgia mobilizes
- Flawless execution and supply-chain consistency across 800+ bottlers is achievable and sustainable over 12 months
- Social signaling around Coca-Cola brand identity has weakened such that rational taste preference dominates tribal/family expectations
