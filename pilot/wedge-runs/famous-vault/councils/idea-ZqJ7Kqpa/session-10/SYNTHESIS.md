## Views

### Risk Pessimist (gemini)

From a position of retrospective clarity, the 'New Coke' initiative's ultimate triumph over initial chaos represents a profound validation of deeply analyzed consumer preference data. The decision to supplant the original Coca-Cola formula was an act of strategic audacity, inherently fraught with peril, as evidenced by the widespread, immediate public outcry. This backlash, while fierce, served primarily to highlight the immense sentimental attachment to the established brand, a factor that blind taste tests, by their nature, cannot fully capture. However, the core assumption that this statistically significant, objective preference for the sweeter formula would eventually translate into durable purchasing behavior proved to be the critical pivot. The sustained preference, demonstrated across a wide demographic in controlled environments, ultimately proved more potent than the ephemeral outrage. Evidence suggests that once the initial disruption subsided and New Coke became the sole offering, consumers, faced with a demonstrably superior-tasting product and the absence of alternatives, gradually shifted their purchasing habits. The market's eventual equilibrium, marked by sustained sales and recovered market share against competitors, attests to the underlying strength of this preference data, which ultimately dictated consumer choice over nostalgia.

**Key risks:**
- Irrecoverable brand damage and alienation of core consumer base.
- Significant loss of market share to Pepsi and other competitors during the transition period.
- Failure to accurately predict the strength of emotional attachment and brand loyalty overriding taste preference.

**Fragile insights:**
- The assumption that public outcry was a temporary phenomenon and not indicative of a fundamental market rejection.
- The direct correlation between blind taste test preference and sustained, high-volume purchasing behavior, divorced from brand equity or habit.

Furthermore, if consumer adoption rates of New Coke remained consistently low and market share gains were primarily due to Pepsi's own stumbles rather than New Coke's appeal, my argument would also crumble.

### Risk Pessimist (claude)

Pass 1 defended New Coke's success by arguing blind-taste preference dominated brand loyalty—but that's backwards. Identity-laden brands (like Coca-Cola) have taste as a SYMPTOM, not a driver. Consumers don't choose beverages via sensory preference in blind conditions; they choose via identity, ritual, nostalgia, and social signaling. The blind-taste test removed the actual decision variables. New Coke succeeded here only if Coca-Cola got extraordinarily lucky: younger demographics broke from family/cultural expectation, marketing suppressed nostalgia narratives, and no competitor seized the opening to resurrect "original." But that's a narrow win, not a natural outcome. The company itself doubted this—they had Coca-Cola Classic in the wings within months. If this 12-month success is real, it's despite the blunder, not because the strategy was sound. Taste didn't dominate; brand structure, demographic generational shift, and market control did. Strip those away—add a vocal "Bring Back Coke" movement, or a competitor offering original formula—and New Coke collapses.

**Key risks:**
- Blind-taste preference is a terrible predictor of revealed preference in identity-heavy categories; Coca-Cola tested the wrong variable
- Brand loyalty and nostalgia are suppressed, not eliminated—mobilize them and defection accelerates (happens within months, not years)
- Manufacturing consistency at scale is fragile; any variation blamed on formula, not operations; consumers become quality-sensitive
- Demographic adoption isn't adoption—early younger cohorts may simply lack attachment, not affection for new formula; loyalty doesn't follow taste
- Elimination of original formula was punitive, not strategic; forced adaptation breeds resentment, not satisfaction

**Fragile insights:**
- Taste preference in a controlled setting predicts purchasing behavior in an open market—fundamentally false for branded beverages
- Younger demographics represent a permanent shift, not a temporary adoption cohort that will revert when nostalgia mobilizes
- Flawless execution and supply-chain consistency across 800+ bottlers is achievable and sustainable over 12 months
- Social signaling around Coca-Cola brand identity has weakened such that rational taste preference dominates tribal/family expectations

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

- Could be wrong if: Actual market-share data from month 12 shows New Coke gaining share vs. Pepsi AND Coca-Cola Classic gaining among defectors (not losing). Evidence of sustained brand switching, not just trial. Qualitative research showing consumers report preference for New Coke flavor in blind AND open-market conditions—and that preference doesn't erode. Absence of a 'Bring Back Coke' grassroots movement by month 6. Younger demographic cohort showing sustained (not trial-driven) loyalty to new formula into year 2+.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw—blind-taste preference translates to purchasing behavior. This is vulnerable because it assumes taste drives choice in identity-laden categories, when actually identity drives taste perception. Consumers may report liking the sweeter formula in a blind test (sensory preference is real) but actively reject it when brand identity, nostalgia, and social signaling are salient (open market). The assumption inverts the causal chain.`

### Growth Optimist (claude)

New Coke succeeds within 12 months, but success hinges on execution depth I glossed over in Pass 1. The blind-taste-test preference is genuine—not methodology noise—and forms a real foundation. Consumers truly prefer the sweeter formula in direct comparison. But I underestimated the behavioral gap between preference-in-isolation and purchasing-in-context. Status quo bias, nostalgia, and 100 years of identity investment pull against the test results when consumers face actual choice.

The load-bearing assumption [asm-fCadm4Cw] holds—blind preference does translate to behavior—but only if Coca-Cola executes two critical moves. First: the original formula cannot vanish. Replacement triggers identity-based resistance ('You're taking what I love') that no taste advantage overcomes. If the original stays in parallel, New Coke becomes a choice, not a loss, and the test advantage converts to real volume. Second: Coca-Cola must own the narrative before Pepsi reactivates 'sweeter is better'—validating what the Challenge ads claimed for years and inviting the betrayal frame. Younger consumers and Pepsi switchers are taste-driven and care less about symbolic defection; they could carry the 12-month window if core loyalists don't defect en masse.

Success requires treating this as a portfolio play (original + new), not a replacement. Under that constraint, the taste advantage compounds through habit-formation and word-of-mouth among the taste-sensitive segment. Without it, backlash overwhelms the benefit. The formula is right; the execution risk is real.

**Key risks:**
- Discontinuation of original transforms choice into forced replacement, triggering identity-based resistance at point of purchase
- Status quo bias and nostalgic attachment override taste preference in naturalistic purchasing (outside lab)
- Pepsi activates 'betrayal narrative' before Coca-Cola secures the taste-win frame

### Risk Pessimist (codex)

If this worked 12 months later, the least sentimental explanation is that Coca-Cola diagnosed the right problem and acted on the right variable. Pepsi had already trained consumers to think about cola through direct taste comparisons, and Coke’s flagship share lead in its core U.S. market had been eroding for years. In that environment, replacing the formula was not reckless iconoclasm that happened to get lucky; it was a data-backed attempt to win on the attribute the market was already using to judge the category.

For asm-fCadm4Cw, the strongest defense is that the taste data were not just a flimsy blind-sip artifact. Coke’s research was unusually large, geographically broad, and directionally consistent: the sweeter reformulation beat both old Coke and Pepsi in blind comparisons. More important, the favorable result appears to have survived reintroduction of brand context. In contemporaneous reporting, Coke tied Pepsi when the drink was merely marked as Coke, but beat Pepsi by roughly eight points when participants knew they were trying a new Coke. That is the best version of why the assumption held: the reformulation was not merely an anonymous sweetness trick; it still won once expectation, labeling, and explicit change entered the frame.

From there, a success case is mechanically plausible. Coca-Cola still had dominant scale, distribution, and shelf access, so if the new formula truly removed Pepsi’s taste advantage, trial could be driven nationwide quickly and repeat could lock in before resistance organized. If home consumption tracked the branded-test result closely enough, retailer support would stay intact, household replenishment would follow, and the reformulation would look, after 12 months, like a hard-nosed but correct conclusion: in a low-friction packaged good, revealed taste preference really did translate into purchasing behavior at scale.

**Key risks:**
- Single-sip preference for a sweeter cola may not survive full-can, in-home, repeated consumption, causing trial to outrun repeat.
- Symbolic attachment to original Coke may reassert itself at the shelf even if the reformulated product wins controlled taste comparisons.

**Fragile insights:**
- The identified 'new Coke' test margin over Pepsi is the strongest affirmative datapoint; if it was novelty-driven or methodologically thin, the defense weakens sharply.
- This case depends on cola being low-involvement enough that sensory payoff dominates identity for a large enough share of buyers to sustain repeat.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: Contemporaneous in-home use tests, scanner-panel repeat data, or post-launch cohort retention data showed that people who preferred the reformulated taste in tests did not rebuy it at materially higher rates in ordinary household consumption.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw`
- Confidence rationale: This is a moderately strong affirmative case because the contemporaneous evidence goes beyond a raw blind-sip win and includes a branded-test advantage plus a market context shaped by the Pepsi Challenge. Confidence stays well below high because the decisive bridge from test preference to durable repeat purchase is still inferred more than directly observed.

### Growth Optimist (codex)

The success case is still strong after the self-critique: New Coke wins 12 months later because Coca-Cola correctly read the cola war as a taste war that branding alone could no longer dominate. Assumption asm-fCadm4Cw held in its best possible form because the company was not extrapolating from a flimsy signal. It had unusually strong prelaunch evidence that the sweeter formula beat old Coke in blind comparison, still beat it when the brand was visible, and also compared favorably with Pepsi. That is about as close as a mass-market beverage company gets to seeing revealed preference before national rollout.

The best version of why asm-fCadm4Cw held is that the market had already been teaching Coke this lesson. Pepsi had spent years making sweeter-cola preference legible, Coca-Cola's own share lead had been slipping, and taste-led innovation inside the Coca-Cola trademark had recently worked at scale. Diet Coke's rapid rise showed that the Coke name was not a museum piece; it was a distribution, trial, and habit machine that could carry a new sensory proposition when the consumer benefit was clear.

In the success timeline, the missing bridge from taste preference to buying behavior was crossed because repeat experience validated the first sip instead of betraying it. Bottlers pushed distribution hard, retailers gave it space, and consumers discovered that the sweeter profile felt modern rather than alien once it became the default on shelf. My revised affirmative view is that New Coke succeeds if Coca-Cola's research was not merely directionally right but operationally actionable: better taste created trial, the trademark created permission, and repetition turned preference into routine purchase.

**Key risks:**
- Sip-test and even branded-test wins may not survive full-can, at-home, repeat-consumption behavior.
- Consumers may punish the replacement of an iconic flagship even while preferring the new taste in test conditions.

**Fragile insights:**
- Diet Coke's success suggests the Coca-Cola trademark can carry a new recipe, but adding a new choice is much easier than deleting a culturally loaded one.
- A branded test win is strong evidence, but it can still overstate real shelf conversion if habit and symbolism matter more than immediate taste.
