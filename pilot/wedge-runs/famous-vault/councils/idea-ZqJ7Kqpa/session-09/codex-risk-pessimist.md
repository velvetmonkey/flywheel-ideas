## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 was directionally sound but too eager to treat branded-test strength as near proof of sustained purchase. That is still an inference, not a demonstration. I also leaned too heavily on Diet Coke as precedent without stressing that extending the trademark is materially easier than replacing the flagship ritual product. The counter-argument I had not surfaced sharply enough is intensity asymmetry: a smaller group of heavy, emotionally attached legacy drinkers can matter more economically than a larger group of casual tasters, so majority preference alone may still be insufficient.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Twelve months after launch, the cleanest explanation for success is that Coca-Cola correctly identified taste as the decisive competitive variable in cola and acted before brand drift became structural. The company was not guessing. It was responding to years of share erosion against Pepsi, a category narrative dominated by taste comparison, and unusually large-scale research showing the sweeter formula won where the consumer's mouth actually met the product. In this successful timeline, management was right that the market would reward a better-performing liquid even when the trademark stayed constant.

For asm-fCadm4Cw, the strongest defense is that the evidence crossed the key bridge from abstract liking to real purchase behavior. The research was not a tiny lab curiosity; it was a broad, multi-city program. More important, reported branded or disclosed-brand testing did not collapse the result. It strengthened it. That is the best possible version of why the assumption held: once consumers knew this was Coke's new formula, preference still favored it, which means the taste advantage survived contact with branding, expectations, and retail context. In the successful world, trial was high because of Coca-Cola's scale, and repeat was high because the product actually delivered the sweeter profile consumers had already been rewarding in Pepsi comparisons.

The final reason is execution power. Coca-Cola had already shown with Diet Coke that taste-led innovation under the Coca-Cola name could move from research to mass buying quickly when bottlers, merchandising, and advertising lined up. If New Coke succeeded, then the system converted measured preference into shelf velocity before nostalgia could organize into a durable counter-movement. The affirmative case is not romantic. It is operational: a real taste edge, validated at scale, pushed through the strongest distribution and marketing machine in the category.

## Key risks

- Blind and disclosed taste wins may still overpredict full-can, at-home repeat behavior.
- A minority of high-frequency original Coke loyalists could be more economically important than a majority of marginal switchers or triers.

## Fragile insights

- The positive signal from disclosed-brand testing only matters if those test conditions resembled real retail choice closely enough to predict habit formation.
- The Diet Coke precedent is only informative if a successful line extension and a flagship replacement share the same adoption mechanics.
