## Revised stance (Pass 2)

Twelve months later, the strongest affirmative read is that Coca-Cola finally converted a research edge into a market win. The company was not solving a branding problem with branding alone; it was upgrading the product experience at the exact sensory dimension where the cola war was being fought. If New Coke succeeded, it did so because management correctly read that colas are a high-frequency, low-friction category where first-sip liking matters enormously, and they had the distribution, shelf presence, and mental availability to turn superior trial into superior repetition.

Assumption asm-fCadm4Cw held in its best form because the blind-test signal was not small, noisy, or anecdotal. Coca-Cola's own historical account says the reformulated formula was preferred in tests of nearly 200,000 consumers, and contemporaneous reporting said the new formula beat old Coke 55 to 45 in blind tests and 61 to 39 when the brand was shown. That is the dream setup for an incumbent: better immediate taste plus the stronger brand name. In the success case, consumers were not being asked to switch into an unfamiliar product architecture; they were simply discovering that Coke now delivered a more rewarding taste while retaining Coke's trust, ubiquity, and habit loop.

The most compelling defense is that taste-preference data translated because Coke did not need perfect rationality, only enough repeat purchase from ordinary drinkers. Later experimental evidence strengthens this mechanism: anonymous tasting and brand-cued choice are different, and Coke's brand cue is powerful. If the liquid now had more of the sweetness profile that won sip tests, while the label still carried the strongest symbolic equity in the category, the open-market result could plausibly be better than either old Coke or Pepsi alone. In that world, the reformulation was not sacrilege; it was product-market fit catching up to the data.

## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 was directionally strong but too smooth. I leaned heavily on the elegant synthesis of better liquid plus stronger brand and did not state plainly enough that the replacement decision, not just the reformulation, was the real hinge. I also relied on later brand-cue research as mechanism evidence; that supports the logic, but it is not direct 1985 proof that loyal Coke households would keep buying after an involuntary substitution.

The counter-argument I underplayed is that open-market cola choice is not just taste plus brand in additive form; sometimes the act of changing the flagship destroys the meaning of the brand itself. The strongest falsifier would be early panel data showing high trial but weak repeat among prior Coke loyalists even when stated taste scores remained favorable.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Twelve months later, this looks like the rare incumbent move that was both analytically correct and strategically brave. Coca-Cola had a flagship brand with enormous distribution but a taste-position problem: Pepsi had taught the market that sweeter cola often wins the immediate comparison. In the success case, Coke stopped defending a legacy recipe as sacred and instead used its scale to put a better-liked liquid behind the most powerful red label in beverages. Once trial became unavoidable across grocery, convenience, vending, and fountain, repeat behavior followed the sensory win.

Assumption asm-fCadm4Cw held because the company did not just discover a lab curiosity; it found a formula that resolved the core split in the cola market. Blind tests had already been signaling that sweeter cola wins on the tongue. Separate cola research shows brand cues can swing visible-choice preference toward Coke. My strongest affirmative read is that New Coke unified both forces: Pepsi-like first-sip appeal plus Coke's shelf trust, habit, and ubiquity. That is exactly the setup where taste-preference data can translate into purchasing behavior at scale.

The best version of why this worked is that consumers were not being asked to learn a new brand, only to keep buying Coke after it became more immediately rewarding to drink. The official Coke history says the reformulation won taste tests with nearly 200,000 consumers, and the broader cola literature shows that brand information materially changes stated preference. Put those together and the upside is large: a sweeter formula that already wins blind comparisons, carried by the brand that already wins the visible market. In that world, twelve months of repeated purchase turns a controversial reformulation into a category reset.

## Key risks

- Open-market identity effects may dominate sip-level preference, breaking the link between blind-test wins and repeat purchase.
- Replacing the original formula instead of running a parallel line could turn a product optimization into a loyalty shock.

## Fragile insights

- The same Coke brand equity that made the change dangerous could, in a success path, be the multiplier that converts a sweeter formula's sensory edge into mass repeat behavior.
