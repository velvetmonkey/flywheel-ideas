## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 was overconfident in taste-based rationality. It assumed blind preference + distribution control = market success. It didn't account for: (1) **Context collapse**: Removing brand identity, nostalgia, and authenticity from the decision environment strips the test of ecological validity. Real purchasing includes all three. (2) **Loss aversion > novelty appeal**: Consumers grieve the loss of the original far more intensely than they celebrate a new sweetness. Psychological attachment to *the original* is the load-bearing thing, not taste optimization. (3) **Weak precedent**: Diet Coke succeeded *as a line extension*, not as a replacement. This assumption ignores that categorical difference. (4) **Identity lock is real**: Soda categories *do* have identity locks, contra Pass 1. Coke's identity is tied to its taste and heritage. Reformulation breaks the pact. (5) **Media amplification**: A vocal minority's backlash gets amplified by cable news and national networks — disproportionate coverage drives perception and purchasing decisions. (6) **Reversibility cost ignored**: If New Coke flops, the cost to revert and rebuild trust is enormous. Pass 1 didn't weigh this downside. (7) **Endowment effect**: The original formula is the endowment; people demand a large premium to give it up — much larger than the taste improvement justifies.
