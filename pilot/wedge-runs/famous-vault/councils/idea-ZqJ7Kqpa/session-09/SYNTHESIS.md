## Views

### Risk Pessimist (claude)

Pass 1 committed a fatal error: conflating sensory preference in an artificial context with behavioral preference in a symbolic one. Blind taste tests measure momentary sensation, not brand loyalty, nostalgia attachment, or loss aversion. The test strips away the very things that make Coke valuable — heritage, ritual, identity — and replaces them with a sweetness score. In the real market, those signals return instantly. Consumers don't just taste; they *remember* what Coca-Cola is supposed to taste like. Changing it isn't an improvement; it's a betrayal. The Diet Coke precedent is misleading: Diet Coke was a new product line, not a replacement. Reformulating the original is categorically different — it says the company was wrong for 99 years. Loss aversion dominates here. People mourn what they lost (iconic taste, authenticity) far more than they celebrate what they gained (sweetness). Vocal nostalgia backlash, amplified by emerging cable news networks, will dominate media and perception for months. Pepsi, locked into position? No — Pepsi wins by *not* reformulating and positioning as the choice of tradition. Switching costs are near-zero only if the product succeeds; if consumers reject it, the cost of reverting (brand damage, manufacturing complexity, time to bring back original formula and rebuild trust) is catastrophic. The 200k blind sample is large statistically but tells you almost nothing about real purchasing under brand-presence conditions. Within 12 months, New Coke becomes a cautionary tale, not a success. The original formula returns (as Coca-Cola Classic), and the company spends years rebuilding trust.

**Key risks:**
- Blind-taste preference collapses under brand-presence conditions; nostalgia and authenticity overwhelm sensory data
- Loss aversion: consumers grieve the original Coke more intensely than they value the new sweetness
- Vocal backlash amplified by media drives perception and purchasing decisions disproportionately
- Original formula becomes cult-status symbol; reversion required within 12 months, destroying brand trust
- Reverting is catastrophically expensive (manufacturing, distribution, brand damage recovery); Coke locked into worst-case path

**Fragile insights:**
- Pass 1's assumption that taste preference in isolation predicts real-market behavior ignores the symbolic dimension of Coca-Cola's identity
- Diet Coke precedent doesn't hold: line extension ≠ replacement of iconic original
- Distribution control and switching costs only matter if the product succeeds; failure makes reversibility the dominant concern
- Taste-optimization framing misses that consumers don't want optimization; they want authenticity and continuity

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: Real market data shows sustained preference for New Coke over 6-12 months post-launch, consumer research indicates authenticity concerns were overblown, brand-loyalty defection stays below 5%, and retailers report stable stock movement. None of these occurred historically.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw — Blind taste-test preference translates to actual purchasing behavior. This assumption conflates sensory preference (measurable, controlled) with behavioral preference (subject to identity, nostalgia, loss aversion, ritual). The test environment is radically different from the purchase environment. Context collapse is the primary failure mode.`
- Confidence rationale: Confidence lowered from Pass 1 (65-70%) to 35 because: (1) the core assumption mistakes a lab signal for a market signal; (2) loss aversion and identity-based consumption are stronger forces than taste optimization in iconic brands; (3) the cost of failure (reverting, rebuilding trust) is asymmetrically high; (4) media amplification of backlash was underweighted in Pass 1. Historical outcome (reversion to Coca-Cola Classic in 79 days) aligns with this pessimist stance. Pass 1 was overconfident in rationality-based taste preference overriding identity-based loyalty.

### Risk Pessimist (gemini)

From a risk-averse perspective, the "new-coke" gambit, while appearing successful on paper 12 months post-launch, was a high-stakes gamble that masked significant underlying brand damage and alienated a critical customer segment. The core assumption that blind-taste-test preference would reliably translate to purchasing behavior proved dangerously optimistic. It failed to account for the powerful, often irrational, influence of brand loyalty, nostalgia, and habit. While New Coke may have achieved parity or marginal gains against Pepsi, this came at the steep price of alienating millions of loyal Coca-Cola drinkers who perceived the change as an affront to a beloved institution. The 'success' is largely a testament to aggressive marketing and sheer market saturation, not to a fundamental, durable consumer preference that supplanted decades of brand equity. The true cost of this decision, measured in lost customer loyalty and diminished brand sanctity, likely outweighs any short-term sales uplift or competitive advantage gained. The survival of the original formula 12 months later is not an indicator of New Coke's inherent success, but rather a pragmatic acknowledgment of the profound market disruption and damage caused by the initial reformulation.

**Key risks:**
- Significant and potentially permanent erosion of core Coca-Cola brand loyalty.
- The "minority" backlash representing a substantial and profitable customer segment whose departure was underestimated.
- Opportunity cost of not focusing on other potential brand enhancements or market strategies that wouldn't risk alienating the customer base.

**Fragile insights:**
- The direct correlation between blind taste preference and open-market purchasing behavior is weakened by the overriding psychological and emotional value consumers place on established brands.
- The perceived competitive advantage against Pepsi is overshadowed by the internal damage to Coca-Cola's own brand identity.

### Growth Optimist (claude)

Pass 1 missed a fatal category error: it conflated blind-taste preference (a signal about isolated flavor) with open-market purchasing behavior (a decision embedded in brand identity and category history). The argument assumed that winning a taste test translates to market dominance because 'taste is the fundamental consumer need.' But for cola—a low-involvement, identity-laden category—taste is one input among many, and brand heritage can outweigh taste advantage when the stakes are identity replacement rather than incremental improvement. The 55% blind-test signal is real, but it's a signal about flavor intensity in a lab setting, not about consumers' willingness to abandon the Coca-Cola they grew up with. The original formula was not losing market share because it tasted worse in isolation—Pepsi's gains came from aspirational positioning and demographic targeting, not blind taste superiority. Reformulating the category-defining product is a category error: it signals doubt about the brand itself, and it removes the optionality the argument claims exists. 'We can always bring back Classic' is not a hedge; it's a backup plan that admits failure. Once you've replaced the icon, heritage consumers don't trust the repositioning—they distrust the company. The assumption fails because it underweights identity, overweights taste isolation, and ignores that Coca-Cola's competitive vulnerability was positioning and segmentation, not flavor. The refutation is historical fact: the outcome disproved asm-fCadm4Cw definitively.

**Key risks:**
- Identity loyalty overpowers taste preference: consumers reject the reformulation not because it tastes worse, but because it signals the brand doubts its own heritage
- The blind test is context-stripped: removing brand cues removes the primary decision-driver in cola purchasing, making the test's signal inapplicable to open-market behavior
- Heritage consumers' backlash cascades: once reputational damage sets in, distribution and marketing cannot recover the emotional trust
- Optionality framing is illusory: reintroducing the original is not a success pivot—it's public admittance that the core bet failed
- Taste is not the binding constraint: Pepsi's market share gains came from positioning and demographic appeal, not superior taste, so taste reformulation addresses the wrong problem

- The argument pivots on 'Coca-Cola's distribution dominance'—but distribution doesn't overcome identity rejection; it guarantees shelf space for a product consumers have decided to reject

In the success timeline, management was right that a better performing liquid under the Coke trademark would arrest share erosion faster than defensive advertising or incremental packaging work.

For asm-fCadm4Cw, the best case is stronger than a generic blind sip story. That is the most persuasive version of why the assumption held. Consumers were not merely fooled by anonymity; they accepted the sweeter profile even when it was presented as Coke's future, which makes it plausible that trial converted into repeat rather than collapsing on contact with the market.

The strongest defense, then, is operational and empirical at once: the data were unusually broad, the brand reveal did not obviously destroy the taste advantage, and Coca-Cola had a recent template for scaling a Coke-branded taste proposition into real buying behavior.

### Growth Optimist (gemini)

The decision to launch New Coke was not merely a product update; it was a visionary stroke of market genius that secured Coca-Cola's dominance for decades to come. By daring to trust sophisticated blind-taste-test data over nostalgic sentimentality, the company not only introduced a superior-tasting beverage but also orchestrated a cultural moment that revitalized brand engagement. The initial public outcry, rather than being a deterrent, became an unexpected accelerant, forcing a national conversation about taste, preference, and brand loyalty. This intense engagement, far from harming the brand, ultimately reinforced its relevance and drove unprecedented traffic to store shelves, proving that even deeply ingrained loyalty can be surpassed by a superior sensory experience when effectively communicated and embraced.

This audacious pivot demonstrated a profound understanding of market dynamics: that while familiarity breeds comfort, innovation driven by clear consumer preference fuels growth. The strategy successfully captured the elusive younger demographic, who gravitated towards the sweeter profile, and re-energized existing consumers by highlighting the brand's commitment to evolving. The subsequent successful reintroduction of 'Coca-Cola Classic' as a distinct, premium option validated the core assumption that the majority's preference lay with the sweeter taste of New Coke, while still acknowledging a valuable niche for the original. This dual-product strategy, born from bold data interpretation, created a more robust and segmented market presence, ensuring Coca-Cola's preeminence against all competitors.

**Key risks:**
- The initial, intense public backlash could have permanently alienated a significant portion of the core customer base, regardless of taste improvements.
- The success relied heavily on the ability to manage the narrative around the 'Classic' reintroduction and its relationship to New Coke, a complex communication challenge.

**Fragile insights:**
- The insight that the public 'outcry' was a net positive for brand engagement is fragile, as it depends critically on the successful adoption of New Coke and the subsequent strategic positioning of 'Coca-Cola Classic'.

### Growth Optimist (codex)

Twelve months later, the strongest affirmative read is that Coca-Cola finally converted a research edge into a market win. The company was not solving a branding problem with branding alone; it was upgrading the product experience at the exact sensory dimension where the cola war was being fought. If New Coke succeeded, it did so because management correctly read that colas are a high-frequency, low-friction category where first-sip liking matters enormously, and they had the distribution, shelf presence, and mental availability to turn superior trial into superior repetition.

Assumption asm-fCadm4Cw held in its best form because the blind-test signal was not small, noisy, or anecdotal. Coca-Cola's own historical account says the reformulated formula was preferred in tests of nearly 200,000 consumers, and contemporaneous reporting said the new formula beat old Coke 55 to 45 in blind tests and 61 to 39 when the brand was shown. That is the dream setup for an incumbent: better immediate taste plus the stronger brand name. In the success case, consumers were not being asked to switch into an unfamiliar product architecture; they were simply discovering that Coke now delivered a more rewarding taste while retaining Coke's trust, ubiquity, and habit loop.

The most compelling defense is that taste-preference data translated because Coke did not need perfect rationality, only enough repeat purchase from ordinary drinkers. Later experimental evidence strengthens this mechanism: anonymous tasting and brand-cued choice are different, and Coke's brand cue is powerful. If the liquid now had more of the sweetness profile that won sip tests, while the label still carried the strongest symbolic equity in the category, the open-market result could plausibly be better than either old Coke or Pepsi alone. In that world, the reformulation was not sacrilege; it was product-market fit catching up to the data.

**Key risks:**
- Open-market identity effects may dominate sip-level preference, breaking the link between blind-test wins and repeat purchase.
- Replacing the original formula instead of running a parallel line could turn a product optimization into a loyalty shock.

**Fragile insights:**
- The same Coke brand equity that made the change dangerous could, in a success path, be the multiplier that converts a sweeter formula's sensory edge into mass repeat behavior.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`
