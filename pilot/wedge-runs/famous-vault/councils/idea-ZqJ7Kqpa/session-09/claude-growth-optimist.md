## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 constructed an internally coherent case, but it rested on a psychological error: treating blind-taste-test data as sufficient to predict behavior in a high-identity, culturally embedded category. The argument stated 'taste is the fundamental consumer need' and 'the data doesn't lie about what consumers prefer'—but it confused the data type (taste preference in isolation) with the decision context (brand replacement in market). The optionality framing ('reposition the original as Classic') is particularly weak on reflection: it frames bringing back the original as a success move, when it's actually insurance against failure. The argument also didn't interrogate why Pepsi's 1970s-80s gains happened if taste was the driver—it borrowed the Pepsi Challenge's success as proof, but that campaign was positioning, not taste-driven market shift. Most critically, the argument never surfaced the core vulnerability: that consumers' purchasing behavior in categories with deep emotional/identity anchors is driven by identity-signaling and heritage, not by isolated taste performance. The blind test measures the wrong thing.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Blind-taste preference translates to purchasing behavior because taste is the fundamental consumer need in cola, and Coca-Cola's research methodology was rigorous enough to surface real preference signals. Fifty-five percent of consumers prefer the new formula in blind tests — that's decisive, not margin-of-error noise. When millions of consumers taste New Coke without brand cues and prefer it over both the original and Pepsi, they're answering the core market question: does this taste good? Once the product reaches consumers through Coca-Cola's unmatched distribution network—vending machines, retail shelf space, food service presence—that preference drives repurchase. The original Coke was already vulnerable; Pepsi's gains over the prior decade prove consumers were willing to switch if offered something better. New Coke delivered that something. Coca-Cola also created optionality: reposition the original as 'Coca-Cola Classic' for heritage enthusiasts while New Coke captures the mass market. The assumption holds because the company solved a real product problem (taste competitiveness) with a solution validated by consumer blind taste buds. Distribution converts preference into market share. The data doesn't lie about what consumers prefer when they taste blind — the assumption is that real consumer behavior in controlled tests predicts real consumer behavior in open markets. Given Coca-Cola's distribution dominance and the clarity of the taste signal, that chain is sound.

## Key risks

- Identity loyalty overpowers taste preference: consumers reject the reformulation not because it tastes worse, but because it signals the brand doubts its own heritage
- The blind test is context-stripped: removing brand cues removes the primary decision-driver in cola purchasing, making the test's signal inapplicable to open-market behavior
- Heritage consumers' backlash cascades: once reputational damage sets in, distribution and marketing cannot recover the emotional trust
- Optionality framing is illusory: reintroducing the original is not a success pivot—it's public admittance that the core bet failed
- Taste is not the binding constraint: Pepsi's market share gains came from positioning and demographic appeal, not superior taste, so taste reformulation addresses the wrong problem
