## Revised stance (Pass 2)

Pass 1 identified the real mechanism, but the defense conflates preference measurement with behavior prediction. The blind-taste data is empirically sound—Coca-Cola genuinely measured a preference shift. The error is assuming this preference persists when the product carries cultural baggage. In the open market, taste preference is not deterministic of repeat purchase because taste is only one driver among identity, habit, and narrative meaning. The mechanism works if narrative stays neutral ("new formula wins blind tests") but breaks if the frame becomes "we're replacing an icon." For 12 months, Coca-Cola has enough distribution power and trial velocity to generate volume. But the critical failure mode is not trial—it's repeat purchase and habit formation. A consumer who prefers the taste blind but then internalizes the narrative "Coca-Cola is changing something sacred to chase youth" may revert to the old formula or switch competitors entirely. The 40-45% who prefer the original in blind tests are likely higher-loyalty, higher-identity-attachment consumers—their vocal rejection could create a narrative spiral that undermines the 55-60% preference signal. Coca-Cola's execution is real. The taste data is real. But the assumption that execution overcomes narrative risk in 12 months is untested and fragile. The blind test is a measurement of *incidence* (what % prefer), not *intensity* (how much do they prefer) or *durability* (will they switch back). That gap is the vulnerability.

## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 erred in treating preference → behavior as mechanical and in over-weighting distribution power relative to narrative risk. The Pepsi Challenge precedent was misapplied: Pepsi never had to make Pepsi *the everyday choice*; it was a marketing campaign that created a moment. New Coke can't retreat—it *must* become the default, which means it must overcome not just preference but identity and switching costs. The 'large, stratified samples' argument is circular: sample representativeness doesn't address whether the preference signal is durable or fragile. If 55-60% prefer the taste blind but only 35-40% have high attachment to taste as a purchase driver, while the remaining 40% are driven by brand identity, habit, and signaling, then preference incidence is decoupled from behavior prediction. The most critical blind spot in Pass 1 is the narrative problem: once New Coke launches, it becomes a *story*, not just a product. That story can invert the sign of preference. A consumer who prefers the taste but resents the change may experience retroactive preference revision—"if Coca-Cola had to change to win blind tests, maybe I should go with Pepsi." The blind test is insulated from this narrative reframing. The open market is not. Coca-Cola's 12-month window is tight for establishing new habit in a daily-consumption category against entrenched switching costs.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

The strongest case for New Coke's 12-month success rests on empirical foundations that even a pessimist must acknowledge are real. Coca-Cola's blind-taste-test data genuinely demonstrates population preference for the sweeter formula—this is measured sensory data, not marketing theater. The Pepsi Challenge in the 1970s proved that blind-test advantage translates to market share gains; Coca-Cola is deploying the identical mechanism with vastly superior resources and distribution power.

In 1985, taste preference dominates cola purchasing behavior in a way it no longer does. Unlike today's fragmented market (diet variants, zero-sugar, functional claims), Coca-Cola is a single-value-prop category. If taste preference objectively shifted in the new formula's favor, purchasing behavior should follow mechanically. Coca-Cola's distribution, shelf control, and marketing execution are unmatched. Even if only 55-60% of the population strictly prefers the new formula blind, the company's market power would drive trial rates high enough to generate volume.

The load-bearing assumption holds under three conditions: the blind-test sample is representative (it was—large, stratified across demographics); taste preference remains the primary purchase driver in 1985 (it does, more than today); and consumer behavior shifts faster than narrative-driven emotional rejection. The mechanism is proven. The data is real. Coca-Cola's execution capability is real.

## Key risks

- Narrative backlash ('we're messing with an icon') reduces repeat purchase velocity despite preference data
- 55-60% taste preference is fragile — driven by narrow demographic slice or artificially high in controlled setting
- Intense loyalty among 40-45% minority drives outsized vocal opposition and depresses trial-to-repeat conversion
- Context collapse: blind preference does not account for identity/signaling costs of switching in open market
- 12 months insufficient for habit formation in daily-consumption category; repeat purchase lags trial

## Fragile insights

- Taste preference dominates cola purchasing in 1985 — true at mean, but preference distribution (intensity) and minority loyalty are unmeasured
- Coca-Cola's distribution power converts trial to volume — true, but confuses trial with repeat; a trial purchase is not a repeat customer
- Pepsi Challenge precedent proves preference translates to market share — misleading; Pepsi never required preference to become default daily choice
- Blind-test sample representativeness validates the signal — true for incidence, silent on durability and intensity; doesn't capture brand-narrative effects
