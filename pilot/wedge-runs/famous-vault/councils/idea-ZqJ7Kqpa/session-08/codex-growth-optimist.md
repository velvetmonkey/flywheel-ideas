## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 leaned a little too hard on early trial as if it were the same thing as durable repurchase. The biggest gap is that sip tests often over-reward sweetness and novelty, while cola loyalty is built in full-can consumption, at-home stocking, and identity. I also underplayed the asymmetry created by a total replacement: even if many consumers liked New Coke, a smaller but more motivated loyalist bloc could still have created outsized churn, retailer noise, and media amplification.

The counter-argument I had not surfaced cleanly enough is that this was not merely a product-improvement test; it was a forced-migration test. asm-fCadm4Cw is most vulnerable because the move asked consumers to do two things at once: prefer the taste and accept the disappearance of the reference point. My revised stance stays affirmative because the evidence could support that leap in a success timeline, but the bridge from preference to durable open-market behavior is less automatic than Pass 1 implied.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

By April 1986, this looks like a win because Coca-Cola correctly diagnosed the core problem: the flagship was losing ground in the one arena that matters most for a cola, immediate taste preference, while Pepsi was turning that edge into supermarket momentum. A sweeter reformulation let Coke answer the Pepsi Challenge on the merits without giving up its distribution, merchandising power, or advertising scale. Once the category leader has the better-tasting liquid and still owns the better system, share recapture can happen surprisingly fast.

The crucial assumption, asm-fCadm4Cw, held because the research signal was both large and increasingly realistic. This was not a flimsy lab result; Coke ran one of the biggest consumer tests in its history, with nearly 200,000 participants overall and a 40,000-person national wave that showed 55% choosing the new formula over old Coke and 52% choosing it over Pepsi. Even better for the bullish case, the preference did not vanish when branding cues entered: when tasters learned they were drinking a new Coke, the reformulation beat Pepsi by about eight points. Best version of reality: people did not merely like a sweeter sip in the abstract; they were willing to grant Coca-Cola permission to modernize its flagship.

The bridge from preference to purchasing also showed up quickly enough to believe the model. Early market data in May 1985 showed Coke sales up 8% versus May 1984, with most of the lift coming from the new drink, and company surveys said 75% of triers would buy it again. In the success path, that early trial converts into habit by late 1985, then into normalized repurchase by early 1986. The result is a cleaner story for bottlers, renewed relevance with younger drinkers, and a category jolt that makes Coca-Cola look bold, contemporary, and once again like the company setting taste rather than defending nostalgia.

## Key risks

- A forced replacement removes the consumer's sense of choice, allowing a smaller but highly loyal minority to create disproportionate backlash and retailer friction.
