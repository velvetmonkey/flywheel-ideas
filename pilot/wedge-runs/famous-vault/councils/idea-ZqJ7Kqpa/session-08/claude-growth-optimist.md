## Revised stance (Pass 2)

Blind-taste-test preference CAN translate to purchasing behavior, but the assumption is fragile because it ignores the context-dependence of preference formation. New Coke could succeed IF: (1) the preference was genuinely strongest in younger demographics driving sufficient revenue to offset older-cohort defection; (2) trial achieved scale through distribution dominance and marketing spend; (3) cultural resistance remained below the threshold to meaningfully suppress repeat purchase. But I was too confident in Pass 1 that identity-driven rejection would remain "vocal minority noise." In cola, identity and heritage are NOT noise—they're the signal. The blind test measures hedonic preference in an isolation chamber; real purchasing happens in social context where "I drink Coke" carries meaning beyond flavor molecules. The deeper vulnerability: I conflated "consumers prefer the taste in a blind comparison" with "consumers will buy and rebuy this product once they know what it is and what it represents." These are categorically different questions. If Pepsi had capitalized, if older consumers had shifted even 5-10% of their household loyalty, if media coverage had created FOMO rather than backlash, the cascade could have been different—but I offered no mechanism robust to these perturbations. The assumption survives only if taste preference was actually so strong in the target segment that it overwhelmed identity signals and heritage sunk-cost effects. Possible, but I hadn't tested it rigorously.

## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 conflated three distinct claims: (a) 'blind taste preference is real' (probably true), (b) 'this preference is predictive of market behavior' (questionable), and (c) 'cultural resistance is a vocal minority that won't suppress purchasing' (almost certainly false). I defended the conclusion without adequately scrutinizing the mechanism. Specifically: I treated the blind test as dispositive evidence, but failed to ask why a test that *removes identity signals* would be predictive of a choice that is *identity-laden*. I also dismissed heritage attachment and cultural narrative as 'noise' when in fact these are the primary drivers of cola loyalty—especially for older consumers with decades of Coke identity. The 'Coke person' identity is a sunk-cost attachment that tastes better, not worse, with time. I should have weighed the possibility that trial saturation doesn't convert to repeat purchase because once consumers know they're drinking a different product, the taste preference reverses under the identity signal. Finally, I underspecified competitive dynamics—Pepsi's failure to capitalize was treated as exogenous, not as evidence that the market was actually quite contested and that Coke's inertia was the only barrier. Remove inertia via cultural backlash, and Pepsi's window opens. All of these point to the assumption being much more fragile than I argued.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

The assumption holds because blind taste-test preference is actually predictive in a hedonic, trial-driven category like cola when you control for demographic target and understand consumer segmentation. New Coke succeeded because (1) the taste preference was real—younger consumers genuinely prefer the sweeter profile and continued buying once the product delivered on the taste promise; (2) identity-driven resistance was a vocal minority, concentrated in older demographics with sentimental attachment to 'the real thing,' not a market-majority signal; (3) trial saturation via distribution dominance and marketing spend converted triers to repeat purchasers, because the product tasted better to the target palate; (4) cola is fundamentally a hedonic product where taste preference outweighs brand archaeology for most buyers, especially younger cohorts without childhood memories anchoring them to the original formula; (5) the 'Coca-Cola Classic' reintroduction as a portfolio product (not a panicked reversal) segmented the market rather than cannibalizing—each product served different consumer motivation (taste innovation vs. heritage), and both grew together. The blind test captured real preference because it isolated taste from the identity narrative that had become noise in the market. Competitors, especially Pepsi, failed to capitalize on the window to steal share—non-execution on Pepsi's side meant Coke's inertia + distribution held. Revealed preference in actual purchasing behavior vindicated the methodology: younger consumers tried New Coke and kept buying it because it tasted better to them.

## Key risks

- Blind test isolates taste; real choice includes identity/heritage/social signaling. These may override preference.
- "Vocal minority" framing too dismissive—opinion leaders in older demographics shape media narrative and trial rates.
- Repeat purchase may crater after trial if consumers choose the product for taste but experience cognitive dissonance because the taste also means 'not real Coke.'
- Portfolio strategy (selling both) assumes segmentation; may instead create confusion and cannibalization.
- Pepsi's non-response was underspecified—even a modest competitive counter-move could have shifted the trajectory.

## Fragile insights

- Taste preference alone predicts purchasing behavior in cola—actually false in identity-driven categories; conflated hedonic appeal with market behavior.
- Identity-driven resistance is a 'vocal minority'—underestimated the opinion-leader effect and the stickiness of cultural narratives in a 12-month window.
- Younger cohorts are taste-preference-dominant buyers—assumed without testing whether younger consumers also carried brand-loyalty baggage or peer-influence dynamics.
- Segmentation via portfolio (both formulas) grows both products—assumed no cannibalization or confusion; ignored that dual-offering may signal weakness/panic.
