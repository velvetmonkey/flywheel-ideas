## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Looking back 12 months from a failed 'new-coke' launch, it's clear our central wager was built on sand. The catastrophic failure stems directly from the misplaced faith in blind-taste-test preference translating to real-world purchasing behavior. Consumers are not disembodied palates; they are creatures of habit, nostalgia, and brand identity. The assumption that a sweeter formula, preferred in a sterile, blind setting, would magically supplant decades of cultural embedding, emotional connection, and routine consumption of 'the real thing' was profoundly naive. The market's rejection wasn't a blip; it was a tidal wave, a visceral backlash against tampering with a cultural icon. The data from blind tests, while seemingly robust, failed to capture the intangible, yet powerful, psychological anchors that bind consumers to their established preferences, particularly with an entity as deeply ingrained in the public consciousness as Coca-Cola. This wasn't just about taste; it was about identity, tradition, and the perceived integrity of a brand.

By solely relying on taste preference data, we ignored the fundamental human tendency to resist change when it disrupts deeply held routines and emotional connections. The 'preference' in a blind test is a pale imitation of the complex decision-making process that occurs in the supermarket aisle, influenced by memory, brand loyalty, and social context. The failure to account for this disconnect means the entire premise of the reformulation was flawed from the outset. The market did not reward a 'better tasting' product; it punished the perceived betrayal of a trusted, familiar entity. The scale of the negative reaction, forcing a swift return to the original formula, underscores how critically this core assumption failed to predict actual consumer sentiment and market dynamics. We bet on chemistry and ignored psychology, a fatal error.

## Key risks

- Underestimating the power of brand loyalty, nostalgia, and cultural embedding over rational product attributes.
- Misinterpreting laboratory preference data as a reliable predictor of real-world market behavior for established, iconic products.
- Ignoring the qualitative aspects of consumer psychology, such as habit and emotional connection, in favor of quantitative taste metrics.
