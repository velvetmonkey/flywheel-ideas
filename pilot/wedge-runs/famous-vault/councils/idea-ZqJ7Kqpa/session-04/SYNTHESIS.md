## Views

### Risk Pessimist (claude)

The declared assumption—taste preference translates to purchasing—is narrower than the failure mode. Blind-taste data was likely *accurate*: New Coke tasted competitively better to most sippers. But Coca-Cola's error wasn't in the taste science; it was in strategy and comms. Discontinuing the original formula was a business decision, not an output of the taste tests. The market failure cascades from that choice, not from taste preference being a false signal. Brand loyalty and emotional attachment *did* override hedonics in purchasing decisions, but I can't attribute that to the taste-preference assumption being wrong—it's orthogonal. The assumption says 'preference translates to purchasing'; it doesn't claim preference is the *only* driver or that it survives discontinuation of the alternative. Within 12 months, the backlash is severe and genuine (core consumers genuinely felt betrayed), but Coca-Cola's rapid reversal to 'Classic' coexistence suggests the core assumption was sound enough to justify the reformulation attempt *in isolation*. The failure was in isolation—it never was isolated. The company bundled the taste bet with a discontinuation bet, and that combination bet lost. Disentangling the two reveals: taste preference probably did drive trial, but brand identity and legacy drove the *rejection of discontinuation*, which collapsed the whole play.

**Key risks:**
- Discontinuation of original formula was a separate bet bundled with the taste bet; the taste assumption's truth-value doesn't determine the combined outcome.
- Core-consumer backlash was real and severe, but it reflects brand-identity dominance *in the full market context*, not falsification of 'taste preference predicts trial.'
- Lack of contingency planning—no reversal-path ready—suggests Coca-Cola underestimated downside, but not that the taste data was mendacious.

**Fragile insights:**
- The blind-taste-test assumption may have been true *and* the decision may have failed, if discontinuation was the failure point rather than taste science.
- Hedonics-vs.-identity is a real tension, but it's orthogonal to whether the test predicted behavior within its intended scope (trial, short-term use).
- Viewing rapid reversal to 'Classic' coexistence as strategic adaptability (not panic admission of error) preserves the idea that the taste data wasn't false—only misapplied.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: The blind-taste-test data *was* genuinely predictive of trial and short-term purchasing behavior, and the failure was entirely attributable to the discontinuation strategy and comms bungling, not to the taste-preference assumption itself. If the company had launched New Coke alongside Classic, market outcomes might have been neutral or positive (two products, market segmentation), vindicating the taste data. This would falsify my Pass 1 stance that the test was a 'catastrophic misread.'

That conflates the declared assumption with the *company's* meta-assumption ('taste preference is the dominant purchase driver'). The declared assumption is weaker and more defensible. ### Risk Pessimist (gemini)

From a risk-averse perspective, the decision to replace the original Coca-Cola formula with 'New Coke', despite favorable blind-taste test results, was an unacceptably high-stakes gamble. The central assumption – that blind-taste preference would directly translate to market purchasing behavior – fundamentally underestimated the inherent risks associated with tampering with a deeply ingrained cultural icon. My primary concern would have been the profound attachment consumers have to established brands, transcending mere taste. The data, while appearing robust, failed to capture the qualitative, emotional, and habitual dimensions of consumer loyalty. This was not just about a beverage; it was about nostalgia, identity, and routine. The potential for a catastrophic backlash, far exceeding mere preference shifts, was palpable. The subsequent market rejection and the hurried return to the original formula serve as irrefutable evidence that this assumption was not just optimistic, but a critical misreading of consumer psychology for heritage brands, prioritizing quantifiable taste over the immeasurable, yet potent, forces of brand equity and consumer habit. We were staring down a cliff, and the taste tests were a distraction.

**Key risks:**
- Underestimating the power of brand loyalty, nostalgia, and cultural embedding over rational product attributes.
- Misinterpreting laboratory preference data as a reliable predictor of real-world market behavior for established, iconic products.
- Ignoring the qualitative aspects of consumer psychology, such as habit and emotional connection, in favor of quantitative taste metrics.

**Fragile insights:**
- Superior taste alone is sufficient to drive consumer purchasing behavior, even for deeply ingrained brands.
- Consumers make purchasing decisions solely based on objective product quality in a market context.

### Growth Optimist (claude)

Pass 1 correctly identified that blind-taste preference doesn't survive removal of choice and brand identity. But I embedded a hidden assumption: *that the formula was actually good*. Here's the harder truth — Coca-Cola conflated stated preference (in a lab, no brand cues) with revealed preference (99 years of consumers choosing the original despite knowing Pepsi was sweeter). That's a category error. If taste truly drove cola purchases, Pepsi would've owned the market already. The original Coca-Cola's 99-year dominance was *evidence against* the taste assumption, not noise. The failure wasn't execution or portfolio strategy — it was epistemic. Coca-Cola weighted recent experimental data over historical market behavior. A parallel-launch strategy *would have been better*, but only because it would have proven the assumption false faster and cheaper. The real growth bet would've been: *If taste doesn't drive behavior at scale, what does? Can we compete on experience, ritual, availability, cultural meaning instead?* Coca-Cola reacted to Pepsi's blind-test wins by chasing a metric that doesn't predict purchasing. That's not an execution miss — it's a misunderstanding of what people buy cola *for*.

**Key risks:**
- Blind-taste data is a poor signal for purchasing behavior in identity-driven categories; 99 years of revealed preference (original dominated despite known taste disadvantage) was a stronger signal and Coca-Cola inverted the epistemology
- Status quo bias + habit + ritual > taste in mature beverages; even a 'sweeter' formula can't overcome a sudden removal of the familiar without catastrophic sunk-cost backlash
- Sample representation and confirmation bias in Coca-Cola's research — were heavy users represented? Did the methodology screen for loyalty effects? Coca-Cola may have commissioned research that confirmed a predetermined outcome
- Parallel strategy would have revealed the assumption false faster, but it assumes the formula was actually good if handled correctly — that might be false

**Fragile insights:**
- Taste matters, but not as the primary driver of cola purchasing — brand identity, history, ritual, and cultural meaning drive behavior; taste is table-stakes, not the lever
- Blind-taste preference under lab conditions (no brand, no choice, no sunk cost) is orthogonal to actual purchasing under market conditions (choice available, identity costs sunk, 99-year habit)
- A portfolio strategy (keeping the original) would have been better, but only because it would have falsified the taste assumption in the real market instead of at the blind-taste bench

### Risk Pessimist (codex)

By April 1986, this has failed because `asm-fCadm4Cw` was false at the exact point the launch depended on it. Blind taste preference did not convert into open-market buying, repeat purchase, or tolerance for permanent substitution. A sweeter sip won a lab-like comparison; the business needed consumers to accept the disappearance of the original, keep buying at scale, and stay emotionally calm. That chain broke.

What the assumption missed is that this was never only a liquid-quality decision. Once Coca-Cola made the replacement visible and irreversible, the market stopped behaving like a taste panel and started behaving like a constituency defending an heirloom. Brand memory, ritual, identity, and the shock of removal dominated sensory preference. In other words, the research isolated the one variable that was easiest to measure and screened out the variables most likely to destroy the launch.

My revised view is slightly narrower than Pass 1: the failure is still centered on `asm-fCadm4Cw`, but the unspoken counter-argument matters. The sweeter formula may have been commercially viable as a line extension, regional test, or optional variant. The catastrophe came from using that assumption to justify replacing the flagship nationwide. So the assumption was not merely inaccurate; it was under-specified for the decision it was asked to carry.

**Key risks:**
- Blind sip-test wins overstate real-world purchase and repeat behavior for a sweeter cola.
- Replacing a legacy flagship converts a taste optimization into a perceived loss event, triggering consumer, retailer, and bottler backlash.

- The main failure mechanism was symbolic loss and replacement shock rather than intrinsic dislike of the new formula; this weakens if open-market repeat purchase data for the new formula was strong.

### Growth Optimist (gemini)

Reflecting on the New Coke saga, my initial Growth Optimist stance correctly identified the pitfall of mistaking laboratory preference for market reality. The robust blind-taste-test data was undeniably exciting – a statistically significant win in pure taste! My early focus was on the potential for market disruption, believing that a superior product would naturally lead to increased sales and dominance. The core belief was that innovation, driven by objective data, was the key to unlocking new growth frontiers. It's a perspective that prioritizes bold moves and the belief that superior product attributes are the primary drivers of success.

However, the catastrophic market response underscores a critical blind spot: I underestimated the profound, often irrational, emotional attachment consumers form with iconic brands. My optimism, while driving me to champion innovation, inadvertently led me to overlook the immense cultural equity and nostalgic value of the original Coca-Cola. The decision to replace it, rather than evolve or augment, bypassed the deep-seated habits and identities tied to the brand. This highlights that while optimizing product taste is a valid growth lever, it cannot operate in isolation from the brand's narrative, cultural resonance, and consumer psychology. True growth often lies not just in creating a *better* product, but in understanding how to *evolve* the consumer relationship with that product.

Looking forward, this painful lesson teaches us that market success is a multi-dimensional equation. While data-driven product superiority is a powerful component, it must be integrated with a nuanced understanding of brand loyalty, emotional connection, and the psychology of change. The opportunity for growth wasn't necessarily lost, but the strategy to capture it was flawed. Future initiatives must therefore balance bold innovation with a deep respect for existing consumer relationships, seeking opportunities to enhance and expand rather than simply replace. This means exploring strategies like product line extensions, iterative improvements, or innovative marketing that leverages nostalgia, rather than a direct, disruptive overhaul of beloved heritage products.

**Key risks:**
- Overemphasis on product attributes (taste) at the expense of brand equity and emotional connection.
- Underestimating consumer resistance to change for highly iconic and nostalgic brands.
- Assuming direct, linear translation from blind-test preference to open-market purchasing behavior.

**Fragile insights:**
- Blind-taste-test preference directly translates to market purchasing behavior.
- Product superiority, as determined by objective metrics, is the sole determinant of market success.

Or, if a subsequent, similar reformulation by another company succeeded, proving the *concept* was viable but Coca-Cola's execution was uniquely poor.

### Growth Optimist (codex)

Twelve months later, I still think there was real commercial signal in the underlying data. A sweeter formula beating both incumbent Coke and Pepsi in blind tests is not trivial; it suggests the team discovered an additive source of demand. The failure, if we work backward honestly, is that we treated a growth clue as a replacement mandate.

Assumption asm-fCadm4Cw is where the case snapped. Blind-taste-test preference did not cleanly translate into real purchasing behavior because real-world buying is bundled with brand memory, ritual, packaging, and the emotional shock of losing the original. Consumers were not just answering which sip they liked better. They were answering whether the company had the right to rewrite a cultural constant, and many likely punished the removal even if the liquid itself scored well.

The counter-argument I underplayed in Pass 1 is that asm-fCadm4Cw may have been partly true and still not sufficient. Taste preference could translate somewhat in-market, yet the all-at-once replacement strategy could still wreck the outcome by converting curiosity into backlash. In that reading, the data supported a new product, but not deletion of the old one.

From a growth-optimist lens, the tragedy is that the upside probably existed in a different shape: line extension, parallel shelf presence, a regional test, or a younger-skewing sub-brand. Failure twelve months later would then read less like proof the sweeter formula was bad and more like proof we chose the most brittle go-to-market design for exploiting a real insight.

- Replacing a beloved default based on context-free preference data rather than observed repeat purchase in-market.
- Interpreting a product signal as permission for brand subtraction instead of portfolio expansion.
