## Revised stance (Pass 2)

By April 1986, this has failed because `asm-fCadm4Cw` was false at the exact point the launch depended on it. Blind taste preference did not convert into open-market buying, repeat purchase, or tolerance for permanent substitution. A sweeter sip won a lab-like comparison; the business needed consumers to accept the disappearance of the original, keep buying at scale, and stay emotionally calm. That chain broke.

What the assumption missed is that this was never only a liquid-quality decision. Once Coca-Cola made the replacement visible and irreversible, the market stopped behaving like a taste panel and started behaving like a constituency defending an heirloom. Brand memory, ritual, identity, and the shock of removal dominated sensory preference. In other words, the research isolated the one variable that was easiest to measure and screened out the variables most likely to destroy the launch.

My revised view is slightly narrower than Pass 1: the failure is still centered on `asm-fCadm4Cw`, but the unspoken counter-argument matters. The sweeter formula may have been commercially viable as a line extension, regional test, or optional variant. The catastrophe came from using that assumption to justify replacing the flagship nationwide. So the assumption was not merely inaccurate; it was under-specified for the decision it was asked to carry.

## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 was directionally right but too tidy. I treated emotional attachment as if it fully explained the outcome, and that risks collapsing two different claims: whether consumers liked the new formula at all, and whether they would accept elimination of the old one. Those are not the same test. I also leaned on backlash as if it automatically proved broad economic failure; vocal protest can be real without being the whole revenue story.

The strongest counter-argument I had not surfaced is that the formula insight may have been useful in a different launch design. If Coca-Cola had kept the original and introduced the sweeter version as an additional product, `asm-fCadm4Cw` might have held for a segment of the market. That does not rescue the 1985 decision, but it does limit how absolute my Pass 1 framing should be.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

By April 1986, the failure traces directly to asm-fCadm4Cw. Management treated a blind sip-test win as if it proved open-market trial, substitution, repeat purchase, and durable loyalty. That is the central error. A sweeter sample beating competitors in sensory isolation does not mean households will tolerate the permanent removal of a 99-year-old flagship.

Asm-fCadm4Cw also failed because it stripped away the context that actually governs purchase behavior. Once branding, packaging, habit, memory, and the disappearance of the original were made explicit, this stopped being a taste decision and became a loss event. Consumers were not simply choosing a cola; they were reacting to the perceived destruction of a cultural object.

The failure mechanics are predictable and severe. Initial curiosity may have created some trial, but repeat purchase weakens under real conditions, complaint volume surges, retailers and bottlers inherit the anger, and the company is forced into a humiliating reversal. The assumption did not miss narrowly; it measured the wrong thing and therefore licensed the wrong launch.

## Key risks

- Blind sip-test wins overstate real-world purchase and repeat behavior for a sweeter cola.
- Replacing a legacy flagship converts a taste optimization into a perceived loss event, triggering consumer, retailer, and bottler backlash.

## Fragile insights

- The main failure mechanism was symbolic loss and replacement shock rather than intrinsic dislike of the new formula; this weakens if open-market repeat purchase data for the new formula was strong.
- The same taste insight might have worked as a line extension rather than a full replacement; this depends on a launch path that was not actually chosen.
