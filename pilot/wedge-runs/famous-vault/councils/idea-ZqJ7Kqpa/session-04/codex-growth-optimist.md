I leaned hard on symbolism without requiring repeat-purchase, household panel, bottler, and regional sales data.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Twelve months later, this looks less like a bad product and more like a bad translation layer from insight to market. We found a formula that wins the sip test, then acted as if that meant it could safely replace a cultural default. Those are different propositions. A sweeter drink can outperform in a controlled comparison and still underperform once people experience the loss of the original in public, habitual, identity-loaded contexts.

Assumption asm-fCadm4Cw is the main break point. Blind-taste-test preference did not translate to actual purchasing behavior because the test stripped away the very things customers buy: familiarity, ritual, label meaning, and the reassurance that the drink they grew up with is still there. The market did not answer the question Which sample tastes better? It answered a harsher one: Why did you take my Coke away? If failure arrives, it is because we confused momentary preference with durable demand and ignored the asymmetry between adding a better option and removing a beloved one.

The frustrating part is that the upside may still have been there. The sweeter formula could plausibly have worked as a line extension, regional experiment, or younger-skewing variant while preserving the original. In that path, we monetize curiosity and choice. In the failed path, we turned a potentially growthy insight into a referendum on brand ownership, and customers used purchase behavior to vote no.
