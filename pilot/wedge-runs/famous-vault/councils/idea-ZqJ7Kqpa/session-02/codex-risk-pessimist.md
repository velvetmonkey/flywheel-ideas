## Revised stance (Pass 2)

By April 1986, I still pin the failure on `asm-fCadm4Cw`, but I need to refine the mechanism. The fatal mistake was not merely trusting taste data. It was trusting taste data gathered in an unreal, consequence-free setting, then using it to justify a national replacement of a 99-year-old flagship. That is how a product test becomes a brand-destruction device.

I attack `asm-fCadm4Cw` explicitly on three grounds. First, the research measured preference, not sacrifice; consumers were not made to fully confront that preferring the new formula meant the original would disappear. Second, the metric rewarded immediate sweetness and abstract liking, not repeated purchase under habit, identity, and memory. Third, management converted a product comparison into a forced-conversion event. Once original Coke was removed, the consumer question stopped being which cola tastes better and became why did you take mine away.

My revised view is narrower and harsher. I am less certain than in Pass 1 that omitted brand cues alone explain the collapse, because contemporaneous reporting says identified tests still favored New Coke. I am more certain that `asm-fCadm4Cw` failed because it assumed open-market, repeated, emotionally loaded behavior would mirror low-stakes test preference. Nearly 200,000 tests created false confidence. The 79-day reversal exposed it.

## Self-critique (Pass 2)

Pass 1 was directionally right but too monocausal. I leaned too hard on the idea that blind testing stripped away brand meaning, yet contemporaneous reporting indicates identified tests still favored New Coke, which weakens a simple brand-cues explanation. The stronger critique is not that consumers could not like New Coke, but that the research did not model the consequence that liking it would erase the incumbent.

I also blurred product failure with replacement failure. A missing counter-argument was that New Coke may have been viable as an added SKU while still being disastrous as a mandatory substitution. That lowers my confidence in a pure formula-rejection story, but not in the claim that `asm-fCadm4Cw` was unsafe as the basis for replacing original Coke.

## Initial stance (Pass 1)

By April 1986, the failure traces straight back to asm-fCadm4Cw. Management treated blind sip-test preference as if it were the same thing as open-market demand. That is the classic research trap: the test stripped away brand, ritual, package, habit, social meaning, and the pain of taking the old product away. In a paper cup, extra sweetness can win a moment. In a supermarket and at the dinner table, people are choosing continuity, identity, and trust.

I would attack asm-fCadm4Cw on three fronts. First, a sip test overweights immediate sweetness and underweights whole-can drinkability and repeat purchase. Second, blind comparison erases the brand cues that make Coca-Cola valuable in the first place. Third, this launch did not merely offer a new formula; it replaced the original flagship, which turned a preference question into a loss-aversion event. Consumers who might have tolerated New Coke as an option instead experienced the move as betrayal when the incumbent was removed.

Once that assumption broke, the rest of the failure became mechanical. Loyal buyers hoarded old inventory, complaints surged, media framed the move as corporate vandalism, retailers inherited confusion, and Pepsi got a free story about Coke abandoning its own heritage. The company paid research, manufacturing, and advertising costs to discover that stated taste preference was the wrong decision variable. This is not a narrow product miss. It is a brand-equity self-inflicted wound caused by overgeneralizing from the wrong evidence.

## Key risks

- Preference research without consequence modeling can create false certainty around irreversible flagship decisions.
- Withdrawing a symbolic incumbent can trigger hoarding, organized protest, retailer disruption, and reputational damage faster than demand data can self-correct.

## Fragile insights

- Branded identity alone may not explain the failure; available reporting says identified tests still favored New Coke.
- New Coke might have survived as a line extension, so the catastrophe may belong more to forced replacement than to the formula itself.
