### Risk Pessimist (claude)

New Coke fails within 12 months. The blind-taste assumption (asm-fCadm4Cw) is fundamentally broken — blind preference does not translate to purchasing behavior in heritage categories where identity and loss aversion dominate taste. However, I may have oversimplified the causal mechanisms. The failure could stem from: (1) taste preference measured in sensory isolation being orthogonal to real-world behavior, (2) loss aversion in beverage switching being lower than my 3–4x estimate (closer to 1.5–2x), (3) the decision-maker misinterpreting market research, or (4) execution being more critical than the formula itself — a gradual rollout with original Coke retained might have softened the shock. The Pepsi Challenge precedent (1970s blind-taste wins, zero market-share gains) is my strongest evidence, but it's not a perfect analogy.

My greatest vulnerability is overconfidence in the inevitability of the outcome. The core assumption (blind preference ≠ purchasing behavior) is almost certainly correct, but I'm less certain about the speed of collapse or whether a different rollout strategy could have mitigated it. If the taste preference were much stronger (>60% sustained in open market) and brand loss aversion in beverages were weaker than I assume, the risk shrinks, though it remains substantial. Media weaponization and competitor capitalization are real, but marketing could plausibly rehabilitate the 'New' narrative if execution were patient and dual offerings were sustained.

Still, Coca-Cola is fundamentally not a taste product — it's a brand, identity marker, and continuity promise. Replacing it based on blind taste remains a category error. The core failure mode is valid. My lowered confidence reflects acknowledged execution risk and uncertainty about loss-aversion magnitudes in this category, not doubt about the basic mechanism.

- Organized vocal minorities weaponize media; competitor (Pepsi) capitalizes; but marketing could rehabilitate 'New' with patience

- Execution is critical: gradual rollout with dual offering counterfactual suggests my 'inevitable collapse' framing was overconfident

### Risk Pessimist (codex)

By April 1986, I still pin the failure on `asm-fCadm4Cw`, but I need to refine the mechanism. The fatal mistake was not merely trusting taste data. It was trusting taste data gathered in an unreal, consequence-free setting, then using it to justify a national replacement of a 99-year-old flagship. That is how a product test becomes a brand-destruction device.

I attack `asm-fCadm4Cw` explicitly on three grounds. First, the research measured preference, not sacrifice; consumers were not made to fully confront that preferring the new formula meant the original would disappear. Second, the metric rewarded immediate sweetness and abstract liking, not repeated purchase under habit, identity, and memory. Third, management converted a product comparison into a forced-conversion event. Once original Coke was removed, the consumer question stopped being which cola tastes better and became why did you take mine away.

My revised view is narrower and harsher. I am less certain than in Pass 1 that omitted brand cues alone explain the collapse, because contemporaneous reporting says identified tests still favored New Coke. I am more certain that `asm-fCadm4Cw` failed because it assumed open-market, repeated, emotionally loaded behavior would mirror low-stakes test preference. Nearly 200,000 tests created false confidence. The 79-day reversal exposed it.

**Key risks:**
- Preference research without consequence modeling can create false certainty around irreversible flagship decisions.
- Withdrawing a symbolic incumbent can trigger hoarding, organized protest, retailer disruption, and reputational damage faster than demand data can self-correct.

**Fragile insights:**
- Branded identity alone may not explain the failure; available reporting says identified tests still favored New Coke.
- New Coke might have survived as a line extension, so the catastrophe may belong more to forced replacement than to the formula itself.

### Growth Optimist (codex)

I still think asm-fCadm4Cw is the center of gravity, but I would sharpen the diagnosis. The company treated one narrow truth as a total market truth: in a blind sip test, many people prefer a sweeter formula. That does not prove they will switch their household routine, accept the retirement of the original, or keep buying once the choice is public, branded, and emotional. If failure shows up twelve months later, asm-fCadm4Cw failed because it compressed trial, substitution, repeat purchase, and loyalty into one taste score.

From my growth-optimist seat, the painful part is that there probably was real upside inside the data. The sweeter formula may genuinely have been better at winning challenges, younger drinkers, or incremental occasions. But the company spent that upside on the most extreme version of the bet: total replacement. That turned a promising product insight into a referendum on identity. A line extension, limited-market rollout, dual-track shelf strategy, or youth-skewed sub-brand could have harvested the upside without forcing loyal buyers to experience the change as a loss.

So my revised failure story is not that the new liquid was necessarily wrong. It is that asm-fCadm4Cw ignored context, and context was the business. The market likely produced curiosity and trial, then weaker repeat than the blind data implied, with backlash magnified because consumers felt something familiar had been taken away. The strongest counter-argument is that bold replacement can sometimes simplify the portfolio and concentrate marketing power; I still think that only works if branded in-market evidence already showed people would tolerate the original disappearing, and the declared assumption does not get us there.

- Blind-test preference generates trial but not sustained repurchase once branding, habit, and loss aversion enter the decision.
- Replacing the flagship converts a product optimization into a perceived removal of a cultural asset, creating backlash that swamps any taste advantage.

**Fragile insights:**
- If the sweeter formula had been launched additively rather than as a replacement, the same taste signal might have produced growth instead of reversal.

The blind taste tests revealed a clear, objective superiority of the sweeter profile – a powerful signal of opportunity. The failure wasn't in the data, but in the execution: we failed to appreciate the profound emotional and nostalgic bond consumers held with the original brand identity. This wasn't just about taste; it was about heritage and self-expression. As a Growth Optimist, I see this not as an end, but as a lesson in *how* to innovate. We can leverage such validated taste preferences to develop complementary product lines, premium variants, or limited-edition offerings that honor existing loyalty while satisfying evolving palates. The data points to a market hungry for novel experiences, and we should be agile enough to deliver them without disrupting core brand equity.

**Key risks:**
- Over-optimism regarding market receptiveness to disruptive innovation for heritage brands.
- Underestimating the intangible value consumers place on brand identity and habit over objective product attributes like taste.

**Fragile insights:**
- Taste preference data is universally applicable to product strategy without deep consideration for brand equity and emotional resonance.
