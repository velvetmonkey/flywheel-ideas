## Views

### Risk Pessimist (claude)

Pass 1 overstated the case. Blind taste tests don't measure brand destruction, but Coca-Cola's failure wasn't inevitable from the data—it was decision-making under genuine ambiguity. The blind test showed New Coke was tastier, and that signal was real. What the test couldn't measure: (1) the *withdrawal* of Classic would activate emotional loyalty as a weapon, not just taste preference degradation; (2) consumers evaluate beverages in *open* markets with brand cues, and Coca-Cola didn't show that secondary validation (or showed weak validation and shipped anyway); (3) Pepsi's gains were real—doing nothing was also a failing bet, just a slower one. The decision's true vulnerability wasn't that taste tests are lies; it's that Coca-Cola *underweighted brand-destruction risk* in a way that competent risk analysis should have caught. The backlash magnitude surprised them, which means their pre-launch testing either missed the downside or their leadership ignored it. That's a forecasting and governance failure, not a fundamental indictment of using taste-test signals. The fact that Classic came back within 79 days shows reversibility they may not have priced in—reducing the decision's stakes in real time.

**Key risks:**
- Brand-destruction risk underestimated in pre-launch testing—psychological backlash was real and larger than forecasted.
- Competitive framing ignored: inaction (keeping original) was losing ground to Pepsi. Reformulation was defensive, not greedy.
- Switching costs measured incorrectly: the issue wasn't taste preference, but the *refusal to let consumers choose*—killing Classic amplified the backlash beyond what the taste data predicted.
- Reversibility discounted or invisible in the original decision framing—79-day correction was available but required crisis management.
- Phase 2/Phase 3 validation data (with branding, in-market awareness) may have been weak or ignored by leadership despite what the data showed.

**Fragile insights:**
- Blind taste tests are 'false oracles'—vulnerable if Coca-Cola's secondary testing with branding intact showed equivalent preference.
- Brand loyalty flips non-linearly—vulnerable if consumer taste preferences were shifting toward sweetness faster than loyalty was eroding.
- Withdrawal of Classic was the real trigger—vulnerable if the backlash was proportional to the taste gap, not the *choice removal*.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: Coca-Cola's Phase 2 and Phase 3 market testing (with branding, in-store shelf, aware consumers) showed equivalent or stronger preference for New Coke over the original. If secondary validation existed and they proceeded despite weak signals, that changes the narrative from 'blind tests failed' to 'they had good data and made a calculated risk that sociological factors and poor communication amplified beyond prediction.' Also falsified if Coca-Cola's internal risk models *did* flag brand-destruction risk and were overridden, in which case it's a governance failure, not a forecasting failure.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw. I haven't interrogated whether Coca-Cola's internal research showed the blind-test preference translating to purchasing in *aware* market conditions. If they ran that test and it held, my entire critique that 'psychology strips away the signal' collapses. The assumption is about prediction (preference → purchase), not about measurement (blind test). I attacked the measurement; I didn't verify the prediction chain.`
- Confidence rationale: Dropped from ~0.78 (Pass 1 implied) to 0.67 because: (1) I acknowledged that Coca-Cola almost certainly had better data than the blind test alone; (2) I credited the competitive desperation—inaction was also a losing bet; (3) I recognized reversibility—79 days to fallback is significant; (4) I identified that execution (naming, removing Classic) was a separate lever from the taste signal. But I'm not moving to 0.5 (uncertainty) because: (1) the decision *did* fail in hindsight; (2) the backlash was real and measurable; (3) blind tests *did* fail to predict behavior, even if Coca-Cola had secondary validation; (4) the downside-weighting was provably insufficient. So: credible that Coca-Cola was more competent than I stated, but credible that the risk analysis was deficient and the decision was a bad bet under the information available at the time.

**Evidence:**

- "Pepsi's market share was gaining in the late 1970s–early 1980s; Coca-Cola's share was declining." — Competitive context documented in beverage industry histories of the period.

### Risk Pessimist (codex)

I remain negative on the decision, but my revised view is slightly narrower: the central mistake is not merely changing a formula, it is forcing a total substitution of an iconic reference product. This is a core-franchise bet with asymmetric downside. If management is wrong, the company does not just miss on growth; it provokes loyal customers, signals that continuity is disposable, and makes a public reversal expensive and humiliating.

I attack asm-fCadm4Cw directly. Blind taste-test preference for a sweeter formula is not a reliable proxy for open-market purchasing after the original is withdrawn. Sip tests mostly capture immediate sweetness response under artificial conditions. They do not capture branded context, full-serving drinkability, habit persistence, identity attachment, or buyer reaction to losing the incumbent. Even very large test samples can be precisely wrong if the construct being measured is the wrong one.

The strongest counter-argument is that Coca-Cola faced real share erosion and had unusually extensive taste data, so caution also carried costs. I accept that. But that only argues for constrained experimentation, not for deleting the benchmark product. When the downside includes franchise damage, the burden of proof for replacement should be far higher than a blind-test win.

**Key risks:**
- Forced replacement of an identity-laden core product triggers backlash, hoarding, protest behavior, and trust erosion among loyal buyers.
- Taste-test overfitting leads management to confuse first-sip sweetness preference with repeat purchase, causing share loss and a costly public reversal.

**Fragile insights:**
- Coca-Cola may be selling continuity and ritual as much as liquid taste; if consumer attachment is weaker or more segmentable than I assume, the downside case is overstated.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: Branded, in-market replacement tests showed high retention and repeat purchase among incumbent Coke drinkers after withdrawal of the old formula, with low complaint volume and no meaningful trust erosion.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw`
- Confidence rationale: Confidence is fairly high because both Coca-Cola's retrospective and independent historical summaries indicate that taste-test wins failed to predict market reaction. It is not higher because I may be overweighting symbolic attachment relative to other explanations such as execution, communications, or channel dynamics.

### Growth Optimist (claude)

Pass 1 missed the forest: I treated the formula as a commodity input and brand identity as orthogonal to it. They're inseparable. Coca-Cola's equity *is* the formula—the emotional contract that 'Coca-Cola tastes like Coca-Cola.' Blind-taste preference is measuring the wrong variable. The Pepsi Challenge spent *years* winning blind tastings without capturing market share; that precedent directly falsifies the assumption that taste preference in isolation drives purchasing behavior. Real consumers don't choose beverages in a blind test—they choose *brands*. If core Coca-Cola loyalists feel betrayed by a formula that doesn't taste like 'theirs,' scale amplifies rejection, not acceptance. You can't distribute your way past identity alienation. The 'cannibalization-managed' recovery thesis assumes the brand can absorb a formula overhaul without losing the core—but the core is *defined* by loyalty to the original formula. Distribution advantage only works if demand exists. Gen X expansion is speculative; nobody tested whether sweeter actually unlocks new segments, or whether Pepsi's growth came from Coke's misstep rather than taste merit.

**Key risks:**
- Core customer base interprets formula change as identity betrayal, not taste upgrade—active rejection rather than trial friction
- Scale amplifies rejection: 90M+ installed base, even 10-15% active rejection = massive word-of-mouth damage and coordinated backlash
- No recovery window: brand damage happens in weeks, not 18 months; by the time data shows problem, cultural narrative is locked (see historical outcome)
- Pepsi precedent: winning blind taste tests is orthogonal to market dominance; Pepsi still didn't capture share despite 20+ years of comparative advantage
- Distribution paradox: logistics excellence at scale only matters if product has demand; forced shelf space for rejected product accelerates retail pressure to delist

**Fragile insights:**
- Assumption that 'nostalgia is separate from the product' misses that brand identity and formula are co-defined for a 99-year-old beverage
- Belief that 'modern taste science positioning' justifies margin expansion ignores consumer logic: they associate science with corporate tinkering, not premium value
- Confidence in 'cannibalization-managed growth' assumes the brand can survive formula identity-rupture—untested and historically falsified by outcome

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: Blind-taste preference actually *does* override brand identity and emotional attachment in purchasing decisions (contradicted by Pepsi Challenge); OR the core customer base doesn't feel betrayed by formula change and views it as innovation (unlikely given historical outcome); OR 'market correction' window is much faster than expected and Coke can re-stabilize before cultural backlash locks in (didn't happen historically).
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw — that blind-taste preference translates to purchasing behavior. This assumption fails because it ignores brand identity, loyalty, emotional attachment, and the Pepsi Challenge precedent. It's the weakest link in the entire decision chain.`
- Confidence rationale: Pass 1 confidence was ~0.65 based on 'data is solid + scale advantage wins.' Pass 2 drops to 0.32 because the data measures taste in isolation, not brand-loyalty purchasing. The Pepsi Challenge is a direct historical falsification of asm-fCadm4Cw. The upside narrative (margin, growth, Gen X) is speculative and rests on false assumption. Core risk (base alienation) is severe and real. Only remaining credibility: 'maybe the brand recovers eventually'—but that's not a win condition for a 1985 launch.

**Evidence:**
- "Pepsi Challenge won blind tastings for 20+ years without capturing lasting market share from Coca-Cola" — historical / competitive record
- "Blind-taste preference is a poor proxy for brand-loyalty purchasing in context; missing brand identity, switching cost, emotional attachment" — consumer psychology / Pepsi precedent
- "Coca-Cola's entire brand equity is built on formula consistency and cultural identity, not taste profile alone" — brand strategy axiom / 99-year brand history

### Risk Pessimist (gemini)

The reliance on blind-taste-test data is a dangerously simplistic approach to a product as culturally entrenched as Coca-Cola. This isn't just about a beverage; it's about nostalgia, habit, and an icon. To assume that a preference in a sterile lab translates directly to market behavior ignores the profound, often irrational, emotional bonds consumers have with their trusted brands. The 'sweeter' formula, however objectively superior in taste trials, risks alienating the very generation of loyalists who view the original as a cultural touchstone. We are not merely altering a recipe; we are potentially eroding decades of brand equity and risking a consumer backlash that could cripple Coca-Cola's identity. This decision, driven by taste metrics, blinds us to the possibility of profound brand damage.

**Key risks:**
- Consumer revolt and boycott due to perceived betrayal of brand heritage.
- Irreversible damage to Coca-Cola's brand equity and cultural status.
- Significant and sustained loss of market share to competitors.
- Long-term erosion of consumer trust and brand loyalty.

**Fragile insights:**
- The assumption that objective taste superiority in blind tests directly correlates with sustained purchasing behavior, ignoring deep emotional and habitual consumer attachments to established brands.

**Assumptions cited:** `asm-fCadm4Cw`

**Metacognitive reflection:**
- Could be wrong if: If consumer attachment to the *idea* of the original Coca-Cola is less potent than their preference for a demonstrably superior taste, and if the market dynamics (e.g., Pepsi's strength) are so dire that *any* change, even a risky one, is preferable to stagnation.
- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw`
- Confidence rationale: The historical failure of New Coke provides strong retrospective evidence that my pessimistic assessment of the risks was accurate, highlighting the primacy of brand heritage and consumer sentiment over laboratory-derived taste preferences in this specific cultural context.

### Growth Optimist (codex)

Pass 1 mostly holds. The taste-test result is real signal, and I still see upside in the sweeter formula itself; if Coke can capture more immediate preference, it can reopen growth against Pepsi instead of just defending heritage. That part of the idea still looks attractive to me.

Where I pull back is on the replacement move. Assumption asm-fCadm4Cw is carrying almost the entire case, and I do not think it is sturdy enough. Blind sip preference measures a moment of anonymous taste; purchasing behavior for Coca-Cola includes label awareness, ritual, habit, identity, and repeat consumption over time. A formula can win the first sip and still lose once buyers know the original is gone.

My revised growth-optimist stance is to chase the upside without forcing a binary break: launch the sweeter formula as an extension, regional rollout, or youth-skewed variant while preserving original Coke. The strongest counter-argument against me is that a half-step could blunt the competitive punch and confuse the market. If branded in-market evidence showed higher repeat purchase, stable penetration, and minimal backlash even after consumers were told the original formula would disappear, I would change my mind and support full replacement.

**Key risks:**
- Replacing the flagship could trigger brand-identity backlash that blind taste tests cannot observe.
- A parallel-line alternative could fragment attention and reduce the speed and clarity of Coca-Cola's response to Pepsi.

**Fragile insights:**
- The cup-test win may matter less than I think once consumers encounter the product as a labeled, symbolic brand rather than an anonymous sample.
- A line extension may preserve upside with limited downside, but that depends on distribution and messaging staying coherent.

**Metacognitive reflection:**

- Most vulnerable assumption: `asm-fCadm4Cw`
- Confidence rationale: Moderate confidence is appropriate because the taste-test evidence is genuinely strong, but the case still relies on a narrow sensory signal carrying over into durable branded behavior, which is a substantial leap.
