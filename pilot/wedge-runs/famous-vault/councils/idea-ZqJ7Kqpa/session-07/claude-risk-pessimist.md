## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Blind taste tests are a false oracle. They strip away every psychological, social, and emotional lever that actually drives beverage consumption. The test isolates the chemical taste signal, but in the real world, consumers buy identity, ritual, and cultural continuity — they buy "Coca-Cola," not a sweetness ranking. You're swapping 99 years of accumulated brand moat (the world's most valuable intangible asset) for a 50-basis-point taste preference measured in a sterile lab. The moment New Coke hits shelves under the Coca-Cola name, the frame changes: it's not "Is this sweeter?" but "Why did they break what I loved?" Loyalty is non-linear — it doesn't decay at the margin; it flips. You're also handing Pepsi and Coca-Cola Classic (if you keep it) a two-front war. The blind test can't measure brand-destruction risk or the power of nostalgia as a competitive weapon.

## Key risks

- Brand-destruction risk underestimated in pre-launch testing—psychological backlash was real and larger than forecasted.
- Competitive framing ignored: inaction (keeping original) was losing ground to Pepsi. Reformulation was defensive, not greedy.
- Switching costs measured incorrectly: the issue wasn't taste preference, but the *refusal to let consumers choose*—killing Classic amplified the backlash beyond what the taste data predicted.
- Reversibility discounted or invisible in the original decision framing—79-day correction was available but required crisis management.
- Phase 2/Phase 3 validation data (with branding, in-market awareness) may have been weak or ignored by leadership despite what the data showed.
