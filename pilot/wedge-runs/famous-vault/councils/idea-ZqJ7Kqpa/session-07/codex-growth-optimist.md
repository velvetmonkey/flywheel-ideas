## Initial stance (Pass 1)

I like the ambition. The taste-test signal is not noise, and a sweeter profile could be a credible offensive move against Pepsi rather than another round of defensive messaging. If the goal is to find growth by improving immediate taste appeal, there is real upside in the reformulation itself.

Assumption asm-fCadm4Cw is the weak link. Blind-taste-test preference does not automatically translate into purchasing behavior when the product is a century-old flagship brand. People do not buy Coke as an anonymous sip; they buy it as a labeled ritual, a habit, and a piece of identity. A sweeter formula can win the cup test and still lose in the cart, in the fridge, and at the dinner table.

My growth-optimist answer is not "do nothing"; it is "change the shape of the bet." Launch the sweeter formula, but do it as a parallel line, regional rollout, or challenger variant while preserving original Coke. That keeps the upside from the taste data while buying the company more ways to win. Replacing the flagship is an unnecessary all-or-nothing move when a portfolio expansion could capture the same demand with less brand trauma.

## Key risks
