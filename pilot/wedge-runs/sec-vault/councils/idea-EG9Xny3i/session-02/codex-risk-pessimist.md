## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Twelve months later, this failed because the core comfort statement behind the filing was too optimistic about shock absorption. The break was not that Apple had no mitigation plan; the break was that the mitigation stack failed under correlated stress. A tariff move, export control expansion, cross-Strait tension, India ramp friction, or a logistics chokepoint did not arrive neatly one at a time. They arrived in clusters, and clustered shocks are exactly where glossy phrases like geographic diversification and multi-sourcing stop behaving like true redundancy.

I would attack [asm-5esVzf6i] directly: the assumption that diversification, multi-sourcing, and dual-supplier programs were sufficient was load-bearing and likely false in practice. "Primarily in China mainland, India, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan and Vietnam" sounds diversified, but several of those nodes are linked by the same regional security environment, shared component dependencies, similar trade-policy exposure, and the same downstream logistics network. Dual-sourcing also tends to fail when both suppliers depend on the same upstream substrate, tooling, advanced packaging, or regulatory corridor. In failure, product availability tightened first, then margins compressed as Apple paid expediting costs, yield penalties, supplier concessions, and inventory rebalancing costs.

The most credible postmortem is not a single catastrophic plant outage; it is a series of medium-sized geopolitical and operational frictions that management believed were containable. The mistake was treating footprint spread as resilience rather than as a partially correlated map of exposure. If this decision failed, it failed because management understated concentration risk hidden inside its own mitigation language.
