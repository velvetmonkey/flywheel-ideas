## Initial stance (Pass 1)

Apple's failure wasn't strategic inevitability—it was a velocity mismatch in a market that shifted faster than their R&D cadence could absorb. The integrated hardware-software stack is a strength, but only if innovation *cadence* exceeds competitor iteration speed. What went wrong: Apple over-indexed on premium markets (China's wealth premium evaporated; US upgrade cycles stretched), under-invested in AI-as-primary-feature (launched Intelligence 18 months after Samsung Galaxy AI went mainstream), and treated Vision Pro as a multi-year bet when the market demanded immediate ROI accountability. The assumption wasn't false—it just had a hidden timing dependency. Competitors didn't leapfrog the integrated model; they copied it faster, and Apple's organizational gravity (product cycles, carrier partnerships, supply chain coordination) became friction instead of moat. The recovery path exists: aggressive software licensing (rent iOS stack to third parties), modular hardware strategy in emerging markets, and ruthless R&D prioritization away from low-probability bets. Apple still has the balance sheet and engineering talent; the failure was optionality, not capability.
